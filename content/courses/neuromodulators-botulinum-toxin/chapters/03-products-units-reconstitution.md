> **Where this fits.** This is the third and final chapter of Part A (Foundations), and it sits here on purpose. Before you ever plan a treatment or study a muscle, you must understand *what you are actually holding in your hand.* Chapter 2 established that a botulinum toxin "unit" measures **biological activity**, not milligrams of drug. This chapter takes that single idea and turns it into working knowledge: the Health Canada–approved type A products, why their units do **not** convert one-for-one, and the principles of storage, reconstitution, and dilution that determine whether a vial reaches your patient safe and effective. We teach these as *concepts and reasoning* — the actual preparation of product for a patient is a supervised, in-person competency, not something learned from a page.

## 3.1 · A Family of Type A Products — Not One Drug

In Chapter 1 you learned to stop saying "Botox" when you mean "neuromodulator." Here is where that discipline earns its keep. Several distinct **botulinum toxin type A** products are approved for use in Canada. They share a mechanism — SNAP-25 cleavage at the neuromuscular junction (Chapter 2) — but they are manufactured by different companies, formulated differently, and, most consequentially, **measured in their own units.**

Think of them as a *family*, not as copies. Members of a family share DNA; they are not interchangeable people. The products below share the type A mechanism; they are not interchangeable doses.

The Health Canada–approved type A products you should recognize by name:

- **onabotulinumtoxinA**
- **abobotulinumtoxinA**
- **incobotulinumtoxinA**
- **prabotulinumtoxinA** (including the brand **Nuceiva**)
- **daxibotulinumtoxinA**

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The precise list of botulinum toxin type A products **currently approved by Health Canada for aesthetic indications**, their exact non-proprietary and brand names, and their approval status must be confirmed against the Health Canada Drug Product Database and each current product monograph before publication. Product availability and indications change; treat every product name, brand, and approval claim here as pending regulatory sign-off.

Notice the naming convention itself is a safety feature. The non-proprietary names were deliberately made distinct — *ona-, abo-, inco-, praba-, daxi-* — precisely so that clinicians and pharmacists would **stop treating the products as interchangeable.** When you see a prefix, read it as a warning label: *this is a specific product, with its own units and its own dosing.*

> 💡 **Read the prefix as a unit system.** Every time you encounter *ona-*, *abo-*, *inco-*, *praba-*, or *daxi-*, mentally append: "…measured in ITS OWN units." That habit is the single most protective reflex in this chapter.

The full side-by-side comparison lives in the product table below (§3.2). Note what the products differ on: the **manufacturer**, the **formulation** (in particular, whether the toxin is bundled with **complexing/accessory proteins** or supplied as the isolated core neurotoxin), the **excipients and storage requirements**, and — the point everything turns on — the **unit basis.**

## 3.2 · Formulation, Complexing Proteins & the Product-Comparison Table

To read the comparison table well, you need one piece of background: **complexing proteins.**

In its natural state, the botulinum neurotoxin does not float free. The bacterium produces it bundled with a set of **accessory (complexing) proteins** that protect the core toxin. Manufacturers differ in how they handle this bundle. Some products retain complexing proteins in varying amounts; at least one is formulated as the **"naked" core neurotoxin** with the accessory proteins removed. This difference is often discussed in relation to **immunogenicity** — the theoretical concern that accessory proteins could contribute to an immune response and, over time, to reduced effect (secondary non-response, which we cover in Chapter 8).

Teach yourself the *concept* and hold the *clinical weight* loosely:

- **The concept is real and worth understanding:** formulations genuinely differ in whether and how much accessory protein they contain.
- **The clinical magnitude is debated:** whether these differences translate into meaningful real-world differences in efficacy, onset, or antibody formation for *aesthetic* dosing is not settled, and you should be wary of marketing that overstates it.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** All claims about complexing-protein content per product, and any statement about immunogenicity or secondary non-response differences between products, must be confirmed against current product monographs and peer-reviewed consensus/reviews. The strength of the immunogenicity claim is contested in the literature — present it as *concept with debated clinical significance*, and cite the specific source before asserting any comparative claim.

## 3.3 · Why Units Are Product-Specific — and Why You Cannot Convert 1:1

This is the central safety concept of the chapter. Slow down here.

**A "unit" is not a fixed amount of drug.** As Chapter 2 established, a unit of botulinum toxin is defined by **biological activity** — historically tied to a potency assay (classically, the amount producing a defined biological effect in a standardized laboratory model). Crucially, **each manufacturer runs its own assay, with its own reference standard and methods.** There is no shared international "botulinum unit" that all products are calibrated against.

The consequence is stark and non-negotiable:

> A unit of one product is **not** biologically equivalent to a unit of another. "20 units" means something different depending on which product produced it. **You cannot convert dose-for-dose between products.**

Learners reach instinctively for a conversion ratio — "so what's the exchange rate between Product X and Product Y?" Resist teaching one as a rule. Ratios discussed in the literature (most famously between onabotulinumtoxinA and abobotulinumtoxinA) are **approximations from specific studies, contested, and context-dependent** — not fixed constants you can apply blindly at the chair.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Any inter-product "conversion ratio" (e.g., ona:abo) must NOT be presented as a usable clinical constant. If a range is mentioned at all, cite the specific comparative studies, state their limitations, and frame it as *why conversion is imprecise*, not as a dosing instruction. Confirm framing with Margie.

Here is how an experienced injector actually thinks about this:

- **Dose in the product you are actually using.** Choose the product first, then use *that product's* monograph- and consensus-informed dosing for the target area. Never plan a dose in one product's "language" and translate.
- **Never carry a number across products.** If your clinic switches products, you do not keep "the same units" — you re-derive the dose for the new product from that product's guidance.
- **Treat a mismatch between product and dose as a stop condition.** A dose that makes sense for one product can be a substantial over- or under-dose in another. If the product on the tray and the dose on the plan don't belong to the same system, you pause and resolve it.

> 💡 **The exchange-rate trap.** The instinct to find a "conversion rate" between products is exactly the instinct to distrust. Units are not currencies with an exchange desk. The safe mental model is: *each product speaks its own language, and there is no reliable translator.* Dose in the language of the product in your hand.

This is not academic. Dose-for-dose substitution errors — treating Product B's units as if they were Product A's — are a recognized source of both **under-treatment** (wasted visit, disappointed patient) and, more seriously, **over-treatment** (excess weakness, ptosis, unwanted diffusion). Everything you learn about complications in Chapter 8 begins with getting *this* right.

## 3.4 · Storage, Cold Chain & Shelf Life — Protecting a Biologic

Botulinum toxin products are **biologics**: proteins whose activity depends on their three-dimensional structure. Heat, agitation, and time can degrade that structure, and a degraded product is not a safe product — it is an **unpredictable** one, which may under-deliver or behave inconsistently. Handling is therefore not housekeeping; it is part of the drug's safety and efficacy.

The principles — learn the *why*, defer the exact numbers to the monograph:

- **Respect the cold chain (where required).** Several products require **refrigeration** within a specified temperature range for the unopened vial; some newer formulations differ. The point is that storage conditions are **product-specific and monograph-defined**, and an excursion outside the specified range can compromise potency.
- **Track shelf life and expiry.** Both the **unopened** shelf life and the **post-reconstitution** window are defined by the manufacturer. A reconstituted vial is not indefinitely usable.
- **Protect the protein from insult.** Avoid excessive heat and vigorous agitation — the same fragility that governs reconstitution technique (§3.5) governs storage.
- **Document the cold chain.** In a real clinic, knowing that a vial has been stored correctly — and being able to show it — is part of accountable practice, tied to lot tracking (§3.7).

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** All specific storage temperatures, unopened shelf-life durations, and post-reconstitution use windows are **product-specific** and must be taken from the current monograph for each product. Do not state any temperature range, hour count, or day count as a general rule. Flag daxibotulinumtoxinA specifically — its formulation and storage may differ from the older products.

> 💡 **"When in doubt about the vial, don't use it."** If storage was uncertain, the cold chain was broken, or expiry/reconstitution windows can't be confirmed, the safe move is the same as with any compromised drug: do not administer, and escalate. A patient never benefits from a vial you cannot vouch for.

## 3.5 · Reconstitution as a Set of Principles (Not a Procedure to Perform)

[[figure: prep-syringe | Reconstitution and dosing are done with fine syringes under strict aseptic technique — but the hands-on *how-to* is a supervised, in-person competency. Here we build the reasoning behind it: diluent choice, volume, and gentle handling of a fragile biologic. |  | Erich Schulz · Wikimedia Commons · public domain]]

Most botulinum toxin products arrive as a **freeze-dried (lyophilized) powder** that must be **reconstituted** — dissolved in sterile diluent — before use. We teach this as *principles and rationale*, deliberately, for two reasons: it builds the judgment that makes the supervised hands-on training meaningful, and preparing product for an actual patient is a competency you earn **in person, under supervision**, not from this page.

> ⚠ **This section is conceptual.** Nothing here is a directive to reconstitute product for a patient. Reconstitution and injection are confirmed through **supervised, in-person practical training** and your clinic's protocols (see the Competency Checklist anchored in Chapter 5). Reproducing steps from a page is not competency.

The principles, and the reasoning behind each:

**1 · Asepsis, because you are creating an injectable.** Reconstitution produces a solution that goes into a patient. It must be prepared using **aseptic technique** — the same non-negotiable asepsis you already practise in nursing — to prevent contamination. This is familiar ground; the aesthetic setting does not lower the bar.

**2 · Diluent choice: sterile, preservative-free vs. preservative-containing saline.** The standard diluent is **sterile normal saline (0.9% sodium chloride)**. There is a long-standing, monograph-versus-practice discussion about **preservative-free** versus **preserved (bacteriostatic)** saline — some evidence and clinical convention favour preserved saline for injection-site comfort, while monographs have traditionally specified preservative-free. Understand the tension; follow the monograph and your clinic's validated protocol.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The diluent specified by each product's monograph (preservative-free vs. bacteriostatic saline), and any evidence cited for reduced injection pain with preserved saline, must be confirmed against the monograph and the specific studies before publication. Do not present preserved saline as monograph-endorsed without checking.

**3 · Gentle handling protects potency.** The toxin protein is fragil​e. The diluent is introduced **slowly and gently down the vial wall**, and the vial is **swirled or rolled, not shaken vigorously**, to avoid foaming and shear stress that could degrade the protein. The rationale — protein fragility — is the same one from storage (§3.4). *(Note: the once-common belief that vigorous agitation destroys the toxin is now debated in the literature; the conservative, monograph-aligned habit of gentle handling remains the teaching default.)*

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The "do not shake" instruction and the evidence for/against agitation harming potency should be confirmed against current monographs and reviews, and framed as *conservative default* if the evidence is genuinely mixed.

**4 · Vacuum, labeling, and time.** Lyophilized vials are often under **vacuum**, which draws diluent in; the vial is then **labeled** (product, concentration, date/time of reconstitution — §3.7) and used within the monograph-defined window (§3.4).

## 3.6 · Diluent Volume, Concentration & the Diffusion Trade-off

Here is where reconstitution stops being a chore and becomes **clinical reasoning.** The *amount of diluent* you reconstitute with determines the **concentration** of the final solution — and concentration, in turn, influences how the product behaves in tissue.

The core relationship:

- **More diluent → lower concentration → larger injection volume** to deliver the same number of units.
- **Less diluent → higher concentration → smaller injection volume** for the same units.

And the clinical consequence that makes this matter:

> A **larger injection volume** (more dilute) tends to **spread/diffuse more** through tissue; a **smaller injection volume** (more concentrated) tends to stay **more localized.**

This is the **concentration ↔ diffusion trade-off**, and it is genuine clinical judgment:

- Where you want the effect **contained** — near a structure you must not weaken, or in a small precise target — a **more concentrated** solution (smaller volume per point) helps keep the toxin where you put it.
- Where a **slightly broader, smoother** effect is acceptable or even desirable across a flat muscle, a **more dilute** solution may be reasonable.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The relationship between injection *volume/concentration* and *field of diffusion* is supported in the literature but its magnitude and clinical importance are debated. Confirm the framing and cite the specific diffusion/volume studies before publication; present as *principle with debated magnitude*, not a precise rule.

> 💡 **How the expert thinks about dilution.** A beginner asks "what's the right dilution?" as if there were one answer. The expert asks: *"What field of effect do I want, how precise must I be, and what's near my target that I must protect?"* Dilution is a lever you set with intention — usually standardized within a clinic for consistency and safety, not improvised per patient.

> 💡 **Standardize to reduce error.** Most safe clinics pick a **standard dilution** and stick to it, so that "units per 0.1 mL" is constant and predictable. Consistency is itself a safety control: it removes a variable and reduces the chance of a math error at the chair.

## 3.7 · Dose → Volume Reasoning: One Worked Example (Educational Reference)

You will plan treatments in **units** (Chapters 5 and 9), but you *administer* in **volume** — millilitres, or fractions of a millilitre per injection point. The bridge between the two is the **concentration** you created at reconstitution. Being fluent in that conversion is a genuine safety skill: it is how you catch an error *before* it reaches a patient.

Let us work one example — framed explicitly as **educational reasoning, not a dosing directive.**

> **Worked example (educational reference — illustrative numbers only; defer to the current product monograph and supervised protocol).**
>
> Suppose a vial contains **100 units** of a given product, and it is reconstituted with **2.5 mL** of sterile saline.
>
> - **Concentration** = 100 units ÷ 2.5 mL = **40 units per mL**, i.e., **4 units per 0.1 mL**.
> - If a treatment plan calls for **4 units** at a given injection point, that point receives **0.1 mL**.
> - If the plan calls for **20 units** across an area, that is **20 ÷ 4 = 5 units-worth of 0.1 mL portions → 0.5 mL** total across that area's points.
>
> Now change *only* the diluent: reconstitute the same **100-unit** vial with **4 mL** instead.
>
> - **Concentration** = 100 ÷ 4 = **25 units per mL**, i.e., **2.5 units per 0.1 mL**.
> - The **same 4-unit point** now requires **0.16 mL** — a *larger* volume for the *same dose*, which (per §3.6) tends to diffuse more.
>
> The units delivered are identical; the **volume and diffusion behaviour are not.** That is the whole point.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The numbers above (100-unit vial, 2.5 mL / 4 mL dilutions, resulting concentrations, and any per-point unit figures) are **illustrative for teaching the arithmetic only.** Confirm that the vial size, dilution volumes, and any dose figures used are consistent with a real product monograph, and caption clearly that actual product selection, dilution, and dosing are individualized and made under medical supervision.

Arithmetic errors are common, silent, and entirely preventable.

## 3.8 · Labeling & Single-Patient Handling

The last principle is unglamorous and absolutely load-bearing: **once you reconstitute a vial, you must be able to say exactly what it is, when it was made, and who it is for.**

- **Label immediately.** A reconstituted vial should be labeled with the **product name, concentration (units per volume), and date/time of reconstitution** — because a clear, colourless solution tells you nothing on its own, and unlabeled syringes are a classic source of error.
- **Single-patient handling.** Handle product in a way consistent with the monograph and clinic protocol for the individual patient; do not create situations where one patient's exposure could reach another. Infection-control and single-patient principles you already hold as a nurse apply fully here.
- **Lot and expiry tracking.** Record the **product, lot number, and expiry** against the patient's treatment record. This is not bureaucracy: it is what makes a recall actionable, a complication traceable, and your documentation defensible (we build the full treatment-record standard in Chapter 9).
- **Discard per protocol.** Beyond the monograph-defined window (§3.4), the vial is discarded — the effect of an out-of-window vial is unpredictable, and predictability is the entire reason we handle biologics carefully.

> 💡 **If you can't name it, you can't inject it.** Any syringe or vial you cannot positively identify — product, concentration, patient, and in-date — is not a syringe you administer. Relabel and re-verify, or discard.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Single-patient-use vs. any multi-dose handling language, and lot/expiry documentation expectations, should be confirmed against the product monographs, infection-prevention standards, and relevant CNO/Ontario documentation expectations before publication.

## Clinical Pearls

- **The prefix is the safety system.** *Ona-, abo-, inco-, praba-, daxi-* exist so you will never treat two products as one. Read every prefix as "…in its own units."
- **Dose in the product in your hand.** Never plan a dose in one product's units and translate it into another's. There is no reliable exchange rate.
- **Dilution is a lever, not a chore.** The volume you reconstitute with sets concentration, and concentration shapes diffusion — a deliberate clinical choice, usually standardized per clinic.
- **Consistency beats cleverness.** A standard dilution, done the same way every time, prevents more errors than any per-patient optimization.
- **Vouch for the vial.** Correct storage, in-date, correctly labeled, correctly reconstituted — if you can't confirm all four, you don't administer.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Hunting for a conversion ratio between products.** The instinct to find "the exchange rate" is precisely the unsafe one — units don't convert 1:1, and any ratio you've heard is contested and context-bound.
- **Treating "units" as a fixed amount of drug.** A unit measures *biological activity*, and each manufacturer defines its own — 20 of one is not 20 of another.
- **Shaking the vial or blasting the diluent in.** The protein is fragile; rough handling risks the very potency you're trying to deliver (and marks you as untrained).
- **Skipping the label.** A clear reconstituted solution looks identical to saline; an unlabeled syringe is a dosing error waiting to happen.
- **Improvising dilution per patient.** Non-standardized dilution multiplies the chances of an arithmetic error and makes results inconsistent. Standardize.
- **Confusing this chapter for permission.** Reading these principles is *not* clearance to reconstitute or inject — that competency is earned in supervised, in-person training.

## Summary

The botulinum toxins used in aesthetics are a **family of distinct type A products** — onabotulinumtoxinA, abobotulinumtoxinA, incobotulinumtoxinA, prabotulinumtoxinA (incl. Nuceiva), and daxibotulinumtoxinA — that share a mechanism but differ by manufacturer, formulation, complexing-protein content, and, decisively, **unit basis.** Because a "unit" measures **biological activity** defined by each manufacturer's own assay, **units do not convert 1:1 between products**, and you must always dose in the language of the product in your hand — the central safety concept of this chapter. These are **biologics**: cold-chain storage, shelf life, and gentle aseptic reconstitution all protect a fragile protein whose activity depends on its structure. The **diluent volume you choose sets the concentration**, which in turn shapes the **concentration ↔ diffusion trade-off** — a deliberate, usually standardized clinical lever — and fluent **dose → volume** arithmetic is how you catch errors before they reach a patient. Finally, **labeling, lot tracking, and single-patient handling** make your practice safe, traceable, and defensible. Everything specific here — every product spec, dose, dilution, and storage figure — defers to the **current product monograph** and is confirmed through **supervised, in-person training.**
