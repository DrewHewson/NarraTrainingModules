This chapter turns that consultation into a *safety decision*: you will learn to take a focused history that actively surfaces the conditions, medications, and expectations that should stop or slow a treatment, to sort them into absolute versus relative bars, and to convert your judgment into a defensible **treat / defer / decline** call backed by valid, well-documented consent. By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

1. **Conduct** a focused aesthetic history that deliberately surfaces contraindications, interacting medications, and unsuitable expectations *(Apply).*
2. **Classify** a patient's findings as **absolute** contraindications, **relative** contraindications, or non-barriers, and explain the reasoning behind each *(Analyze).*

## 7.1 · The Focused Aesthetic History: Screening as an Active Skill

A safe injector does not *wait* for a contraindication to declare itself. She goes looking for it. The single most common way a beginner gets into trouble is not a shaky hand — it is a history that was too shallow to surface the thing that should have stopped the treatment.

Think of the pre-treatment history as having a job: to answer four questions *before* you ever pick up product.

- **Is there a reason the muscle-weakening effect could be dangerous for this person?** (neuromuscular conditions, pregnancy/lactation)
- **Is there a reason the product itself could harm them?** (prior reaction to a botulinum toxin product or its components, active infection at the site)
- **Is there something that will change the safety, the result, or the bruising risk?** (interacting medications, bleeding risk, prior aesthetic history)
- **Is there a reason their *expectations* make this an unsafe treatment to give?** (body dysmorphic disorder, unrealistic goals — carried forward from **Chapter 6**)

Notice that only the first two categories tend to *stop* a treatment outright. The second two usually *shape* it — a deferral, a modified plan, a longer consent conversation. Holding that distinction in your head as you take the history is what separates screening from box-ticking.

> 💡 **Ask the question that the form doesn't.** Intake forms catch what patients think to declare. Your job is to catch what they don't. "Any muscle or nerve conditions, even mild ones?" surfaces more than "Are you healthy?" And "What medications *and supplements* are you on?" surfaces the fish oil and the antibiotic the patient never thought to mention.

A structured history for neuromodulator screening should deliberately probe: **neuromuscular and neurological conditions; pregnancy and breastfeeding status; current medications and supplements** (with specific attention to agents affecting neuromuscular transmission and to blood thinners); **allergies and any prior reaction to a botulinum toxin treatment**; **bleeding tendency**; **skin integrity and active infection at or near the planned sites**; and **the psychological and expectation screen** you learned in Chapter 6. The sections that follow give you the reasoning to interpret what you find.

## 7.2 · Absolute Contraindications: When You Do Not Treat

An **absolute contraindication** is a finding where the safe answer is *no treatment*, full stop — not a modified plan, not a smaller dose. These are few, and you should know them cold, because the whole point is to recognize them before product is drawn up.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Every specific contraindication named in this section is safety-critical. The categories below reflect the standard consensus and product-monograph framing, but the exact list, wording, and the *absolute-vs-relative* classification of each item (particularly pregnancy/lactation and specific neuromuscular disorders) must be confirmed against current Health Canada product monographs and a current upper-face consensus source before this chapter ships. Do not treat any single item here as final.

**1. Known hypersensitivity or a prior reaction to a botulinum toxin product or its components.** If a patient has had a genuine hypersensitivity reaction to a botulinum toxin preparation — or to a component such as the human albumin some products contain — that product (and potentially the class) is off the table. A prior reaction is the clearest possible signal.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm the specific excipients (e.g., human serum albumin) listed as contraindication triggers against each Health Canada–approved product's current monograph before naming them to learners.

**2. Active infection or compromised skin at the injection site.** You do not inject through infected, inflamed, or broken skin — you risk seeding infection and you cannot properly assess the tissue. This is typically a reason to **defer** until the site heals rather than to decline the patient forever, but at the moment of treatment it is an absolute bar to injecting *there, then.*

**3. Neuromuscular disorders that impair transmission at the neuromuscular junction.** This is the category that most demands your respect. Botulinum toxin works *by* impairing neuromuscular transmission; in a patient whose transmission is already compromised, even a small, well-placed cosmetic dose can produce an exaggerated or dangerous effect — including on muscles you never targeted.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The conditions in this category — including **myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome, and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)** — and whether each is framed as an absolute contraindication or a "treat only under specialist direction" relative one, must be confirmed against current monograph and consensus wording. Escalate any such history to the prescriber; a cosmetic clinic is not the setting to make this call independently.

> 💡 **Why the NMJ conditions sit at the top of the list.** Everything you learned about mechanism in **Chapter 2** is the reason here. The drug's entire action is to reduce acetylcholine-driven muscle activation. A patient whose junction is already failing has no reserve to lose. This is the cleanest example in the module of *mechanism predicting risk.*

**4. Pregnancy and breastfeeding.** Cosmetic neuromodulator treatment is generally **avoided** in pregnancy and lactation.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Pregnancy/lactation is classified here as a treat-avoidance based on **caution and consensus in the absence of adequate safety data**, not on proven fetal harm. Confirm the precise framing (absolute contraindication vs. "avoid — insufficient data") against current monographs before publication, and confirm how it should be worded to learners. Cosmetic treatment is elective and deferrable, which is itself part of the reasoning: there is no urgency that could justify accepting an unquantified risk.

The unifying logic of this list: **you decline when the risk cannot be reduced by modifying the plan, and the treatment is not worth accepting that risk.** Cosmetic work is elective — that fact does real work in every decision on this page.

| Finding | Class | Default action | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Known hypersensitivity / prior reaction to a toxin product or a component | **Absolute** | Decline | The clearest possible signal of harm |
| Active infection or compromised skin at the site | **Absolute** *(there, then)* | Defer until healed | Risk of seeding infection; the tissue can't be assessed |
| Neuromuscular disorder impairing NMJ transmission (e.g. myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton, ALS) | **Absolute** *(escalate)* | Decline / prescriber-directed only | No transmission reserve — mechanism (Ch 2) predicts an exaggerated effect |
| Pregnancy / breastfeeding | **Avoid** *(caution; insufficient data)* | Defer | Elective and deferrable — no urgency that justifies an unquantified risk |
| Anticoagulant / antiplatelet use / bleeding tendency | **Relative** | Treat with counseling | A bruising risk, not a safety bar — never stop their prescription yourself |
| Unrealistic expectations / active BDD | **Relative → often decline** | Decline or defer | Treatment can't deliver what's sought; risk of harm |
| Prior facial asymmetry, dry eye, or facial-nerve history | **Relative** | Modified plan / prescriber input | Certain treatment patterns could aggravate it |

## 7.3 · Relative Contraindications & Shared Decision-Making

A **relative contraindication** does not forbid treatment; it *raises the bar.* It means: proceed only if the benefit clearly justifies the added risk, the plan is modified appropriately, the consent conversation is fuller, and — often — the prescriber is looped in. Relative contraindications are where clinical judgment actually lives, and where shared decision-making with the patient becomes central.

Common relative considerations include a **bleeding tendency or anticoagulant/antiplatelet use** (a bruising caution — see §7.4), **a history of dry eye or prior facial-nerve issues** that certain patterns of treatment could aggravate, **unrealistic expectations**, and the psychological screen carried from Chapter 6.

**The expectation and psychological screen is a contraindication, not a soft factor.** A patient with active **body dysmorphic disorder (BDD)**, or with fixed unrealistic expectations that no amount of counseling shifts, is a patient for whom treatment is likely to *harm* — because the treatment cannot deliver what they are actually seeking. From Chapter 6 you already have the red-flag screen; here is where it changes a decision.

> 💡 **"Relative" is not a synonym for "go ahead."** Beginners hear *relative* and reach for the product. Experts hear *relative* and reach for a conversation — with the patient, and often with the prescriber. The finding hasn't disappeared; it has become something you must actively manage and document.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm the specific items placed in the *relative* column — particularly any autoimmune or neuromuscular-adjacent conditions and any history of prior facial asymmetry or nerve palsy — against current consensus, and confirm which warrant mandatory prescriber consultation before treatment.

Shared decision-making here has a specific meaning: you disclose the *specific* added risk this patient carries, you check that they understand it, you document that exchange, and — crucially — you retain the right to decline even if the patient wants to proceed. Consent runs one direction only: a patient can refuse a treatment you offer; a patient cannot *authorize* a treatment your judgment says is unsafe.

## 7.4 · Clinically Significant Interactions

Two categories of medication matter here, and the beginner error is to confuse them — to treat a *bruising caution* as an absolute bar, or to wave through a genuine *neuromuscular* interaction.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The interacting agents, the direction and magnitude of each interaction, and the recommended action must all be confirmed against current product monographs and a pharmacology reference before publication. Interaction claims are safety-critical and evidence-sensitive.

**Agents that affect neuromuscular transmission — the interaction that matters most.** Certain drugs can *potentiate* botulinum toxin's effect, meaning they may deepen or prolong the muscle-weakening action, sometimes beyond the intended site. The classic example is the **aminoglycoside antibiotics** (e.g., gentamicin), which themselves interfere with neuromuscular transmission.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm the full list of neuromuscular-transmission-affecting agents to name (aminoglycosides and other classes such as certain other antibiotics, some muscle relaxants, and anticholinesterases), and confirm the recommended action — typically deferral until the interacting course is complete, or prescriber consultation. Do not present the list as exhaustive without verification.

The reasoning, again, is mechanism: if the drug's job is to reduce neuromuscular transmission and the patient is *already* on something that reduces it, the effects can stack unpredictably. The usual safe response is to **defer** cosmetic treatment until the interacting medication is finished, or to escalate to the prescriber — not to quietly reduce the dose and hope.

**Anticoagulants and antiplatelets — a bruising caution, not an absolute bar.** Blood thinners (whether prescribed anticoagulants/antiplatelets or over-the-counter agents and supplements that affect bleeding) do **not** contraindicate neuromodulator treatment. What they do is raise the risk of **bruising** — a cosmetic and comfort issue, not a safety catastrophe for a superficial upper-face injection. The right response is *not* to cancel and *not* to tell a patient to stop a prescribed blood thinner on your own authority. It is to **counsel about the higher bruising risk, document that counseling, use bruise-minimizing technique** (covered with the rest of complication prevention in Chapter 8), and never adjust a patient's prescribed medication yourself.

> 💡 **Never tell a patient to stop a prescribed blood thinner for a cosmetic treatment.** That decision belongs to the prescriber who put them on it, weighing risks — like stroke or clot — that dwarf a bruise. If bruising risk genuinely concerns you, defer the elective treatment; do not touch the prescription.

| Medication class | Effect on the toxin | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Aminoglycoside antibiotics (e.g. gentamicin) & other agents affecting neuromuscular transmission | May **potentiate** — a deeper or prolonged effect, possibly beyond the target | **Defer** until the course is complete, or escalate to the prescriber |
| Anticoagulants / antiplatelets / bleeding-affecting supplements | Raise **bruising** risk only — *not* a safety bar | **Counsel**, document, use bruise-minimizing technique; never stop their prescription yourself |

## 7.5 · The Treat / Defer / Decline Framework

Every screening ends in one of three decisions. Naming them makes you deliberate instead of default, and gives you language to justify the call in the record and to the patient.

- **Treat** — no absolute contraindication; any relative factors are identified, discussed, appropriately managed, and documented; expectations are realistic; consent is valid. You proceed with a plan (Chapter 9).
- **Defer** — the barrier is *temporary or resolvable*: an active infection at the site, an interacting antibiotic course still running, a patient who needs more time to consider, or a pregnancy. You are not saying "never" — you are saying "not today," and you say *when* to return.
- **Decline** — an absolute contraindication is present, or a relative one you judge unmanageable (fixed unrealistic expectations, active BDD, a neuromuscular history the prescriber will not clear). You decline kindly, clearly, and — where appropriate — with a referral or an explanation of what would need to change.

Two principles hold this framework together.

**When in doubt, escalate — don't improvise.** The safe move when a finding is ambiguous is almost never to proceed with a modified plan you invented on the spot. It is to pause and consult the prescriber / medical director. A cosmetic timeline never justifies an independent call on a genuine safety question. This is exactly the accountability point from **Chapter 1**: working under a directive does not transfer your judgment — confirming the patient truly meets the directive's conditions is *your* job, and "the directive let me" is not a defence for treating someone you should have deferred or declined.

**The default under uncertainty is the more conservative option.** Defer beats treat; decline beats a doubtful treat. You can always treat a well-screened patient next week. You cannot un-inject.

> 💡 **The pre-treatment pause.** Before product is drawn up, ask yourself the same one-line question every time — the sibling of Chapter 1's authorization check: *Is there anything in this history, these medications, or these expectations that should make me defer or decline?* If you cannot answer a confident "no," you have your answer: pause.

## 7.6 · Valid Informed Consent

A signature is not consent. **Consent is a conversation** that a signature merely records. In Ontario, for consent to a treatment to be *valid*, it must satisfy a small number of non-negotiable elements — and because cosmetic patients are well people accepting a risk for an elective benefit, the bar for disclosure is arguably *higher*, not lower, than in therapeutic medicine.

> ⚠ **Verify against current CNO & Ontario law (Margie / regulatory sign-off).** The consent elements and standards below reflect the framework as generally understood under Ontario's **Health Care Consent Act** and **CNO** consent standards. Every specific legal claim — the required elements, the standard for material-risk disclosure, and documentation requirements — must be confirmed against current CNO practice standards and Ontario legislation before publication. This section is legally consequential.

Valid consent rests on four pillars.

**1. Capacity.** The patient must be able to understand the information relevant to the decision and appreciate the reasonably foreseeable consequences of consenting or refusing. For routine cosmetic treatment in a well adult this is usually straightforward — but capacity is decision-specific, and you confirm it rather than assume it.

**2. Disclosure of specific and material risks.** This is where cosmetic consent is most often too thin. Generic "risks include bruising" boilerplate is not enough. The patient must hear the **material** risks — the ones a reasonable person in their position would want to know — *specific* to what you are treating: bruising and swelling; headache; the possibility of **asymmetry**, an **unintended heavy brow, or eyelid or brow ptosis** if the effect spreads or the dose is misjudged; the possibility of an **under- or over-correction**; and the fact that a **touch-up or review** may be needed. These are the very complications Chapter 8 teaches you to recognize and manage — consent is where you name them *before* they can happen.

**3. Voluntariness.** The decision must be the patient's own, free of coercion or undue pressure — including the subtle commercial pressure of a clinic that profits from the treatment, or a promotion with a deadline. A patient who feels rushed has not truly consented.

**4. Realistic outcome and the no-guarantee framing.** The patient must understand what the treatment *can* and *cannot* do, that results are **temporary** and vary between people, that **no specific result is guaranteed**, and that this is medicine, not a purchase with a warranty. This framing does double duty: it protects the patient from disappointment and protects you from a complaint built on a promise you never should have made.

> 💡 **Consent is a process, not a page.** The most defensible consent is a documented *conversation* in which the patient asked questions and you answered them — captured in your note — not just a form with a signature at the bottom. If it wasn't discussed, a signed form doesn't prove it was.

### Documentation to a Medico-Legal Standard

If it isn't written down, it didn't happen — that is the working assumption of every regulator and court. Your documentation is the durable evidence of every good decision you made in this chapter. At minimum, the record should capture: the **focused history and the findings** (including the pertinent negatives — that you *asked* about neuromuscular disease and blood thinners and the answer was no); the **relative contraindications discussed and how they were managed**; the **treat/defer/decline decision and its rationale**, including any prescriber consultation; the **specific risks disclosed and the patient's understanding**; the **consent obtained**; and (from Chapter 9) the treatment details and standardized photography. Contemporaneous, specific, and honest beats lengthy every time.

## Clinical Pearls

- **Screening is an active search, not a passive form.** The contraindication that hurts a patient is the one nobody asked about. Ask the questions the intake form doesn't.
- **Mechanism predicts risk.** The reason neuromuscular conditions and NMJ-affecting drugs top the danger list is the same reason the drug works at all — it impairs neuromuscular transmission. When you understand Chapter 2, this chapter's dangers stop being a list to memorize and start being obvious.
- **Blood thinners bruise; they don't bar.** Confusing a bruising caution with an absolute contraindication is a classic beginner error — and telling a patient to stop a prescribed anticoagulant is a serious one.
- **Consent runs one way.** A patient can decline what you offer; a patient can never authorize what your judgment forbids.
- **The conservative option is almost never the wrong one.** You can always treat next week. Defer and decline are clinical decisions, not failures.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Treating "relative" as "proceed."** A relative contraindication is a trigger for a fuller conversation, a modified plan, and often a prescriber consult — not a green light.
- **Taking a shallow history to save time.** Every minute skipped in screening is borrowed against a complication you'll spend hours managing.
- **Improvising around a genuine safety question instead of escalating.** Inventing a reduced-dose "compromise" for a patient with a neuromuscular history or an interacting drug, rather than deferring or consulting the prescriber.
- **Confusing a signature with consent.** A signed form with no documented conversation is thin protection and, worse, often means the patient wasn't truly informed.
- **Promising a result.** "You'll look completely refreshed with no lines" is a guarantee you cannot keep and a complaint waiting to happen. Frame outcomes as realistic, variable, and temporary.

## Summary

Safe neuromodulator practice begins before any product is drawn up, with a **focused history** that actively surfaces contraindications, interacting medications, and unsuitable expectations. **Absolute contraindications** — known hypersensitivity or prior product reaction, active infection at the site, neuromuscular disorders that impair junction transmission (e.g., myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton syndrome, ALS), and pregnancy/lactation — mean *no treatment*; you decline or defer. **Relative contraindications** raise the bar and demand shared decision-making, a modified plan, a fuller consent conversation, and often prescriber involvement — including the BDD and unrealistic-expectation screen carried from Chapter 6. Among **interactions**, agents affecting neuromuscular transmission (such as aminoglycosides) can potentiate the toxin and warrant deferral or escalation, while anticoagulants and antiplatelets are a **bruising caution to counsel, not an absolute bar** — and you never stop a patient's prescribed blood thinner yourself. Every screening resolves into **treat, defer, or decline**, with escalation and the conservative option as your defaults under uncertainty. Finally, **valid informed consent** — capacity, disclosure of specific and material risks, voluntariness, and a realistic no-guarantee framing — is a documented *conversation*, recorded to a **medico-legal standard**, not a signature on a form. Every specific clinical and legal claim in this chapter awaits the evidence and regulatory sign-off flagged throughout.
