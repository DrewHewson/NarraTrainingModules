> **Where this fits.** This is the third chapter of Part B (Applied reasoning), and it is where the anatomy finally meets a person. In **Chapter 4** you learned to read the upper face as elevator/depressor pairs and to separate **dynamic** rhytids (lines of movement) from **static** rhytids (lines etched at rest). In **Chapter 5** you learned the principle-level logic of each treatment area. This chapter teaches you to run the encounter that comes *before* any of that: how to sit with a patient, elicit what they actually want, decide whether they are a suitable candidate, read their face systematically, and translate what you see into treatment **targets** — all documented to a standard that protects the patient *and* you. Nothing here is a needle skill. It is the judgment layer, and it is where good injectors are separated from careful ones.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

1. **Structure** a client-centred consultation that elicits the patient's real motivation, sets honest expectations, and includes a graceful way to say "no" *(Apply).*
2. **Screen** for body dysmorphic disorder and other unsuitable motivations using recognizable red flags, and decide when to defer or decline *(Analyze).*
3. **Perform** a systematic facial assessment — facial thirds, symmetry, brow and lid position, resting tone and muscle strength — and distinguish dynamic from static rhytids at the chair *(Analyze).*
4. **Translate** assessment findings into specific, defensible treatment **targets**, documenting pre-existing asymmetry as a baseline *(Analyze).*
5. **Apply** the principles of standardized clinical photography as both a clinical tool and a medico-legal safeguard *(Apply).*

## 6.1 · The Consultation Is the Treatment

Beginners think the consultation is the paperwork you get through on the way to the syringe. Experienced injectors know the opposite: **the consultation is where the outcome is decided.** By the time you pick up a needle, the two decisions that most determine whether this patient will be happy — *should I treat this person at all?* and *what am I actually treating?* — have already been made. The injection just executes them.

So slow down. A good aesthetic consultation does four things, in roughly this order:

- **Understands the person** — not just the line they point to, but *why* it bothers them and what they imagine changing.
- **Assesses the face** — systematically, at rest and in motion, documenting what is already there.
- **Sets honest expectations** — what a neuromodulator can and cannot do for *this* face, framed before any commitment.
- **Reaches a shared, documented decision** — treat, defer, or decline — with valid consent (built in full in **Chapter 7**).

> 💡 **The consultation mindset.** You are not a salesperson meeting a customer; you are a clinician meeting a patient who happens to be well. Your job is not to convert an enquiry into a booking — it is to determine what, if anything, is in this person's genuine interest, and to be honest about it. The clinics with the best reputations decline more than beginners expect.

A useful way to hold the whole encounter in your head is a simple flow. You will see the visual version as figure **M1-12**; here it is as an outline you can run in your mind.

| Stage | What you are doing | The question you are answering |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Open** | Warm, unhurried greeting; open-ended invitation to talk | "What brings you in, in your own words?" |
| **2. Elicit** | Draw out the *real* motivation, history, and mental picture of success | "What is it you'd like to change, and why now?" |
| **3. Screen** | Listen and probe for red flags — unrealistic goals, external pressure, BDD signals | "Is this person likely to be helped by treatment?" |
| **4. Assess** | Systematic facial analysis, at rest and in motion (§6.3) | "What is actually here, and what is treatable?" |
| **5. Educate & align** | Explain honestly what treatment can and cannot achieve for *this* face | "Do their expectations and my assessment match?" |
| **6. Decide** | Treat / defer / decline, as a shared decision | "What is the right, defensible next step?" |
| **7. Document & consent** | Photography, findings, plan, consent (Ch 7) | "Is this recorded to a medico-legal standard?" |

Notice that **the needle appears in none of these stages.** That is the point of this chapter.

[[figure: m1-12-consultation-flowchart | The aesthetic consultation, end to end — open, elicit, screen, assess, educate, decide, document. The needle is deliberately absent; every judgment that matters is made before it appears. | M1-12]]

## 6.2 · Eliciting the Real Motivation — and the Honest "No"

### Open questions before closed ones

The single most common consultation error beginners make is **leading**: "So you're here for your frown lines?" The patient nods, and you have just replaced *their* concern with *your* guess. Open the encounter with genuinely open questions and let the patient fill the space:

- "What would you like to talk about today?"
- "Tell me what you see when you look in the mirror."
- "What made you decide to come in *now*?"

That last question — *why now?* — is quietly one of the most revealing in aesthetic medicine. A wedding in six weeks, a recent divorce, a comment from a partner, a milestone birthday, a job loss: the trigger behind the visit tells you a great deal about whether the patient's expectations are anchored to their *face* or to something treatment cannot touch.

### Find the goal beneath the request

Patients ask for treatments; they *want* outcomes. Your task is to translate. A patient who says "I want Botox in my forehead" may actually mean "I look angry when I'm not," or "I look exhausted," or "I want to look like I did five years ago." Those are different goals with different — sometimes unachievable — implications. Reflect back what you hear and confirm it: *"So if I understand you, it's less about the lines themselves and more that you feel you look tired even when you're rested — is that right?"* When the patient feels understood, expectation-setting becomes collaboration rather than correction.

### Set expectations before you commit to anything

Honest expectation-setting is a safety behaviour, not a courtesy. For neuromodulators specifically, patients must understand *before* treatment:

- Onset is not immediate (days to peak effect — quantified in **Chapter 2**), and the result is **temporary** by design.
- Neuromodulators soften lines of **movement**; lines already etched **at rest** (static rhytids) will improve only partially, and some may need other modalities you do not provide (from **Chapter 4**'s dynamic-vs-static framework).
- Faces are **asymmetrical**, and treatment reveals — sometimes accentuates — asymmetry that was always there (§6.4, and Case Study 2).
- The goal in this course's philosophy is **natural**, not frozen; conservative dosing and a review is a feature, not under-delivery.

> 💡 **Under-promise on the visit, over-deliver on the result.** It is far easier to add at a two-week review than to walk back an expectation you set too high. Frame conservative treatment *as* the standard of care — because it is — not as a limitation you are apologizing for.

### The "no" conversation

You will meet patients you should not treat: unsuitable candidates, unrealistic goals, wrong timing, or a request outside your scope or the directive's conditions. Declining well is a core clinical skill, not a failure of one. A graceful "no" is:

- **Specific and honest, not evasive.** "I don't think a neuromodulator will give you the change you're describing, and I'd rather tell you that than take your money for a result you won't be happy with."
- **Non-judgmental.** You are declining a *treatment plan*, not the person.
- **Constructive where appropriate.** Offer the genuine alternative — a different modality, a referral, a reassessment after a life event settles, or a physician/NP review.
- **Documented.** Record what you were asked, what you found, why you declined or deferred, and what you advised. A well-documented "no" is one of the most protective entries in a chart.

## 6.3 · Screening: Body Dysmorphic Disorder and Unsuitable Motivations

Some patients should not be treated not because of their *face* but because of their *relationship to it*. The most important of these to recognize is **body dysmorphic disorder (BDD)** — a psychiatric condition in which a person is preoccupied with a perceived flaw in appearance that is slight or not observable to others, causing significant distress or impairment. It matters enormously in aesthetics because people with BDD frequently seek cosmetic procedures, are characteristically **not** satisfied by them, and can deteriorate afterward. Treatment does not fix the underlying distress; it can feed it.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The clinical description of BDD, its estimated prevalence among cosmetic patients, and the statement that cosmetic procedures generally do not improve (and may worsen) BDD outcomes are all citation-sensitive. Confirm the definition against current diagnostic criteria and the prevalence/outcome claims against the screening literature (see References) before publication. Do **not** state a specific prevalence figure until it is verified.

You are **not** diagnosing BDD — that is outside your scope, and diagnosis belongs to appropriately qualified professionals. Your job is narrower and entirely within it: to **notice the pattern, decline or defer treatment, and refer or escalate** to the supervising physician/NP and, where appropriate, toward psychological support. Screening is pattern recognition plus a low threshold to pause.

### Red-flag checklist

Watch and listen for the following. No single item is diagnostic; a *cluster* should stop you and prompt escalation. This is figure **M1-13**.

| Domain | Red flag to notice |
|---|---|
| **The concern itself** | Preoccupation with a flaw you can barely or cannot see; the distress is out of proportion to any finding |
| **Time & impairment** | Reports spending long periods checking, mirror-avoiding, or camouflaging; the concern interferes with work, relationships, or daily life |
| **The story** | Multiple prior procedures with persistent dissatisfaction; "no one has ever got it right" |
| **Expectations** | Wants a specific, exact, often unachievable outcome; believes the procedure will fix life problems (relationship, career, self-worth) |
| **Motivation & pressure** | Here because someone *else* wants it; or driven by acute life crisis rather than a settled wish |
| **Interaction style** | Fixation on tiny details; brings heavily edited photos of themselves or others as the target; dismisses your reassurance |
| **After the fact** | (On review visits) never satisfied regardless of result; escalating requests; anger disproportionate to outcome |

> 💡 **How experts hold this.** Screening is not interrogation. Most of these signals surface naturally if you simply ask open questions and *listen* — which is exactly why §6.2 comes first. A patient who cannot name a realistic goal, who is here for someone else, or whose distress dwarfs the finding in front of you, is telling you something important. Believe them and slow down.

[[figure: m1-13-bdd-redflag-checklist | BDD and unsuitable-motivation red-flag checklist — a clustering tool, not a diagnosis. A cluster of flags means pause, decline or defer, and escalate/refer; it does not mean you diagnose. | M1-13]]

### Other unsuitable motivations (not BDD)

Not every "no" is about BDD. Also defer or decline when you see:

- **External pressure** — a partner, parent, or employer is the real driver.
- **Crisis timing** — treatment sought in the acute aftermath of grief, breakup, or job loss; revisit when things settle.
- **Perfectionism about a normal face** — a demand to erase all asymmetry or all movement, which conflicts with a natural result.
- **Scope or directive mismatch** — the request falls outside your authority or the directive's conditions (recall the authorization question from **Chapter 1**).
- **Medical unsuitability** — a contraindication surfaces; this is the handoff to **Chapter 7**, which builds the full treat/defer/decline framework and consent.

> ⚠ **Verify (regulatory).** Any statement about the obligation to refer or escalate suspected mental-health concerns should be aligned with current **CNO** standards on the therapeutic relationship, scope, and referral before publication.

## 6.4 · Systematic Facial Assessment

Now the face. Assess **systematically** and **the same way every time** — a fixed routine is what stops you missing the pre-existing droop that later gets blamed on your treatment. Assess both **at rest** and **in animation**, because that contrast is the whole basis of neuromodulator planning (from **Chapter 4**).

### Facial thirds and the upper-face focus

A standard way to organize the face is into vertical **thirds**. It orients you, calibrates proportion, and — importantly — keeps you honest about what is and isn't in your treatment scope (this module treats the **upper face**).

[[figure: assess-facial-thirds | The face in vertical thirds: upper (trichion → glabella), middle (glabella → subnasale), lower (subnasale → gnathion). The **upper third** is your treatment zone. Landmarks: tr = trichion, n = nasion, sn = subnasale, gn = gnathion. |  | Al-Sebaei, *Head & Face Medicine* 2015 · PMC · CC BY 4.0 · ⚠ verify + Margie sign-off]]

| Third | Boundaries (approx.) | Upper-face relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Upper third** | Hairline (trichion) → glabella (brow line) | Frontalis and forehead lines; brow height and shape — **your primary treatment zone** |
| **Middle third** | Glabella → base of the nose (subnasale) | Contains the periorbital region; lateral canthal lines ("crow's feet") sit at its upper-lateral edge |
| **Lower third** | Subnasale → base of the chin (menton) | Named for context only; **out of scope** for this module |

> 💡 The thirds are a *proportion and orientation* tool, not a rulebook — real faces vary, and "ideal" ratios are guides, not targets to impose. Use them to structure your eye, then treat the face in front of you.

### Symmetry, brow, and lid position — read the baseline

No face is symmetrical. Your job is to **notice, measure by eye, and document** the asymmetry that is already there *before* you treat — because whatever you don't record, you may later be accused of causing.

Assess, systematically:

- **Brow height and shape**, left vs. right. Is one brow already lower or higher? Is the arch peaked or flat? (This directly changes targets — see Case Study 2.)
- **Upper eyelid position and any pre-existing ptosis.** Look for a resting droop, hooding, or dermatochalasis (excess upper-lid skin). Distinguish a *lid* droop from a *brow* droop — a distinction you will need again for complications in **Chapter 8**.
- **Compensatory frontalis activity.** Some patients unconsciously use the frontalis to hold heavy or hooded brows up. Weaken that muscle without recognizing its job and the brow can drop — a classic beginner trap. Ask yourself: *is this forehead working to keep the eyes open?*
- **Resting symmetry of the glabella and periorbital region**, and any facial-nerve asymmetry or old palsy.

### Muscle strength and resting tone

Beyond position, assess **how the muscles behave**:

- **At rest (resting tone):** where are lines present *without any movement*? These are **static** rhytids. A line etched at rest will only *partially* soften with a neuromodulator, because the neuromodulator addresses movement, not the etched crease. Say so out loud during expectation-setting.
- **In animation (muscle strength):** have the patient raise the brows (frontalis), frown/scowl (glabellar complex — corrugators and procerus), and squint/smile (orbicularis oculi, lateral canthal lines). Watch the **strength and vectors** of pull. A strong, heavy frontalis, a deep dynamic glabellar frown, or a powerful lateral orbicularis each imply different targeting and dosing *concepts* (executed conceptually per **Chapter 5**; individualized under supervision).

### Dynamic vs. static — the pivotal distinction

This is the assessment finding that most changes your plan, and it comes straight from **Chapter 4**:

| | **Dynamic rhytid** | **Static rhytid** |
|---|---|---|
| **When visible** | Only on movement | Present at rest, deepens on movement |
| **Underlying cause** | Muscle contraction creasing skin | Repeated creasing + skin/collagen change over time |
| **Response to neuromodulator** | Softens well — the core indication | Partial improvement at best; the etched component remains |
| **Expectation to set** | "This should soften nicely" | "This will improve but not disappear; other treatments target the etched line" |
| **Assessment method** | Watch the line during animation | Look at the face fully at rest, muscles relaxed |

> 💡 **The at-rest photo is your honesty check.** Static lines that you promised would vanish are the number-one source of disappointed patients. If a line is deep *at rest*, name that limitation during the consult — never at the two-week review.

### From findings to targets

Assessment is only useful if it becomes a **plan**. Translate what you found into *conceptual* targets — which muscles, and why — recognizing that specific dosing and injection execution are individualized and taught/performed under supervision (**Chapters 5 and 9**). This mapping is the bridge:

| Assessment finding | Muscle(s) implicated | Conceptual target / consideration |
|---|---|---|
| Horizontal forehead lines on brow-raise | **Frontalis** (sole brow elevator) | Soften dynamic lines *without* over-weakening — frontalis holds the brow up; treat cautiously to avoid brow ptosis (Ch 5, Ch 8) |
| Vertical "11s" / scowl between brows | **Corrugators, procerus** (depressors) | Core glabellar target; balance against elevators (Ch 4 elevator/depressor pairs) |
| Fanning lines lateral to the eye on smiling/squinting | **Lateral orbicularis oculi** | Superficial, stay lateral — avoid zygomaticus/smile asymmetry (Ch 5) |
| Heavy, hooded brow held up by an active forehead | Compensatory **frontalis** activity | Caution: weakening frontalis may drop the brow — may **defer** or treat conservatively |
| Line still present at rest (static component) | (as above) + skin change | Set expectations: partial improvement; other modalities for the etched line |
| Pre-existing brow/lid asymmetry | Varies by side | **Document as baseline**; may treat sides asymmetrically to balance (Case Study 2) |

This target map feeds directly into **Chapter 9**, where you build the fully documented, patient-specific treatment plan; the contraindications that can veto any target are built in **Chapter 7**.

## 6.5 · Standardized Clinical Photography

Photographs are not a marketing afterthought. They are a **clinical assessment tool** (they reveal asymmetry the naked eye glides over) and a **medico-legal record** (they prove the baseline you found and documented). Their value collapses if they are inconsistent — a different angle, light, or expression between visits makes any comparison meaningless and can create the *appearance* of a change you didn't cause. **Consistency is the entire discipline.**

### The principles that make photos usable

- **Consistent lighting.** Even, front-facing, shadow-free illumination; the same setup every time. Shadows fake or hide lines.
- **Consistent background and framing.** Plain, uniform background; same distance and head position; hair off the face; makeup considerations noted.
- **Consistent angles.** A defined set of views captured the same way each visit (see shot list).
- **Consistent expression sets.** Both **at rest** and a fixed set of **animations** (raise brows, frown, squint/smile) — because dynamic-vs-static lives in exactly this contrast.
- **Neutralize variables.** Remove glasses; tie back hair; note recent makeup, sweating, or skincare that could alter appearance.

### The shot list

Capture, at minimum, the following — the same list at every visit so images are comparable over time:

| Shot | View | Expression |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Frontal | At rest (relaxed) |
| 2 | Frontal | Brows fully raised (frontalis) |
| 3 | Frontal | Frown / scowl (glabellar complex) |
| 4 | Frontal | Squint or full smile (lateral canthal / orbicularis) |
| 5 | Left oblique (45°) | At rest |
| 6 | Right oblique (45°) | At rest |
| 7 | Obliques | Repeat key animations if area-relevant |

> 💡 **Shoot before you treat — every time, no exceptions.** The pre-treatment set is worthless if it isn't captured before the first injection. Build it into your routine so it is never skipped for a "quick top-up." The patient who returns unhappy about an asymmetry is the patient whose baseline photo proves it pre-existed.

### Privacy and consent for images

Clinical photographs are **personal health information.** They must be:

- **Consented specifically** — a general treatment consent does not automatically cover taking, storing, or (especially) *using* images for teaching or marketing. Consent to *take* and consent to *share* are separate permissions.
- **Stored securely** — access-controlled, retained and eventually disposed of per applicable privacy law and clinic policy.
- **Handled per Ontario privacy law** for personal health information.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / regulatory).** The privacy handling of clinical images (consent categories, storage, retention/disposal) must be aligned with current **Ontario personal-health-information legislation (PHIPA)** and **CNO** documentation standards, and the consent language coordinated with **Chapter 7**, before publication. Do not assert specific retention periods until verified.

## Case Study 1 — Patient Selection

> **The encounter.** *Priya, 52*, books for "forehead Botox." She is warm, articulate, and clearly troubled by horizontal forehead lines and a "tired, heavy" look around her eyes. On open questioning she says she has always had "hooded" eyes but they seem "worse" lately. On assessment you note **mild pre-existing upper-eyelid hooding (dermatochalasis)** bilaterally, and a **visibly active frontalis** — her forehead is working, low-grade, even at rest, subtly holding her brows up. Her forehead lines are largely **dynamic** but with an early **static** component. There are no BDD red flags: her goal is realistic ("I'd like to look less tired, not different"), the timing is unremarkable, and she takes your explanations on board. The one complication is that heavy, hooded brow held up by an active frontalis.

**The decision: treat, defer, or decline?**

Work it through the way an experienced injector would:

- **Is this person suitable?** Yes — realistic goal, no red flags, no obvious contraindication surfaced (formal contraindication screening is **Chapter 7**). Candidacy on motivation grounds is fine.
- **What's the specific risk here?** This is the crux. Her frontalis is **doing a job** — compensating for hooded, heavy brows. The frontalis is the *only* brow elevator. If you weaken it to erase her forehead lines, you may unmask the heaviness and **drop her brows**, making her look *more* tired and hooded — the exact opposite of her goal. This is a **relative contraindication** to aggressive frontalis treatment, not an absolute one.
- **So what's the defensible move?** Not a flat decline, and not treating as requested. The judgment call is to **treat conservatively and selectively**, or **defer part of the plan**, after explicit expectation-setting — and to document the reasoning. Concretely: prioritize the **glabellar** target (safe, high-yield), treat the **frontalis lightly if at all**, place injections to respect brow support, set the expectation that some forehead lines will remain to protect her brow position, name the **static** component that won't fully clear, and **review at two weeks** before considering more. If she cannot accept remaining forehead lines, that is a signal to defer rather than chase a result that risks her brows.

> ✅ **Guided debrief.** The beginner error is binary — *treat what she asked for* or *decline her.* The expert move is **selective, staged, and conservative**, driven by a single anatomical insight: *this forehead is holding these brows up.* Notice the chain: open questioning surfaced the "heavy/tired" motivation → systematic assessment caught the compensatory frontalis and the hooding → that finding converted a routine request into a **relative contraindication** requiring altered targets and honest expectation-setting. Notice too that "defer" was never "no forever" — it was "not this part, not today, let's review." This is the treat/defer/decline reasoning that **Chapter 7** formalizes and **Chapter 9** turns into a documented plan.

> ⚠ **Verify (clinical).** The specific claim that an active frontalis compensating for brow ptosis/hooding constitutes a relative contraindication to frontalis chemodenervation should be confirmed against upper-face consensus/technique literature (see References) before publication.

## Case Study 2 — Assessment

> **The encounter.** *Marc, 44*, is a straightforward candidate for glabellar and lateral canthal treatment — no red flags, realistic goals. On your systematic assessment you notice something he has not mentioned and does not seem aware of: his **right brow sits visibly lower than his left at rest**, and on brow-raise the **left frontalis lifts more strongly** than the right. This is a **pre-existing brow asymmetry** — almost certainly long-standing and unrelated to anything you might do.

**Why you document it, and how it changes your targets.**

- **Document it first, before you touch him.** Photograph the resting and animated sets (§6.5), and write the finding into the chart in plain terms: *"Baseline: right brow ~lower than left at rest; left frontalis stronger on elevation. Discussed with patient."* This is not busywork. Facial asymmetry is *universal*, but patients rarely notice their own until a treatment "reveals" it — and then they attribute it to the treatment. **Your baseline record is the difference between "as we discussed, this was pre-existing" and an indefensible complaint.**
- **Tell the patient now, not later.** Point it out gently at the mirror: most people have some brow asymmetry and have simply never looked. Setting this expectation pre-treatment converts a potential grievance into a sign that you are thorough.
- **Let it change the targets.** Symmetry is not always achieved by treating both sides identically. A pre-existing asymmetry may call for **asymmetric planning** — for example, adjusting placement or the conceptual dose between sides to *balance* rather than *mirror*, and being deliberate about how glabellar treatment interacts with each brow's resting position and each frontalis's strength. (Specific dosing is individualized and executed under supervision — **Chapters 5 and 9**; the elevator/depressor balance behind this is **Chapter 4**.) The principle: **treat the asymmetry you documented, don't ignore it and hope.**

> ✅ **Guided debrief.** Two habits carried this case. First, a **systematic** assessment caught an asymmetry the patient never mentioned — a fixed routine finds what an ad-hoc glance misses. Second, the injector **documented and disclosed the baseline before treating**, which is simultaneously good care and the strongest medico-legal protection you have. The finding then flowed into **asymmetric targeting** rather than reflexive symmetric dosing. Baseline asymmetry is not a problem to hide; it is information to record, disclose, and plan around.

## Clinical Pearls

- **The consultation decides the outcome; the needle just executes it.** If you are unsure at the end of the consult, you are not ready to inject.
- **Ask "why now?"** The trigger behind the visit reveals whether expectations are anchored to the face or to something treatment cannot fix.
- **A cluster of red flags means pause, not diagnose.** You screen and refer; you do not diagnose BDD. When in doubt, defer and escalate.
- **What you don't document, you may be accused of causing.** Photograph and record every pre-existing asymmetry, droop, and static line *before* you treat.
- **The at-rest view is your honesty check.** Static lines set the expectations that dynamic results can't rescue later.
- **A well-documented "no" is one of the safest entries in a chart.** Declining well is a clinical skill, not a lost sale.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Leading the patient** ("Here for your frown lines?") instead of asking open questions and hearing the real motivation.
- **Selling the treatment they asked for** rather than assessing whether it serves them — and treating the consult as paperwork.
- **Over-promising**, especially on static lines, then facing a disappointed patient at the two-week review.
- **Missing the compensatory frontalis** — weakening a forehead that was holding heavy brows up, and dropping the brow.
- **Treating both sides identically** in the face of pre-existing asymmetry, and mirroring a flaw instead of balancing it.
- **Skipping or improvising photography** — inconsistent images that prove nothing, or no pre-treatment set at all.
- **Treating suspected BDD "just this once"** because the patient is persuasive, instead of declining and referring.

## Summary

The consultation, not the injection, is where the outcome is decided. Run it as a structured flow — **open, elicit, screen, assess, educate, decide, document** — and use **open questions** (especially *"why now?"*) to reach the patient's real motivation and set **honest expectations** before any commitment, including a graceful, documented **"no"** when treatment is not in the patient's interest. **Screen** for body dysmorphic disorder and unsuitable motivations by recognizing a *cluster* of red flags; you notice, decline or defer, and **refer/escalate** — you do not diagnose. Assess the face **systematically and identically every time**, at rest and in animation: facial thirds for orientation, brow and lid position and symmetry, resting tone and muscle strength, and the pivotal **dynamic-vs-static** distinction (from **Chapter 4**) that governs what a neuromodulator can achieve. Translate findings into conceptual **targets**, always **documenting pre-existing asymmetry as a baseline**. Finally, treat **standardized photography** as both clinical tool and medico-legal safeguard — consistent light, angle, and expression sets, captured *before* treatment, consented and stored per privacy law. This assessment feeds contraindications and consent in **Chapter 7** and the documented plan in **Chapter 9**.
