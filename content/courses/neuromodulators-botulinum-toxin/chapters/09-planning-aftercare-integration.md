Everything until now taught you a *part*: the science (Chapter 2), the products (Chapter 3), the anatomy (Chapters 4–5), the consultation (Chapter 6), the contraindications and consent (Chapter 7), and the complications (Chapter 8). By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

1. **Synthesize** a structured facial assessment into a documented, patient-specific treatment plan with an explicit rationale for every target *(Create).*

6. **Reason** the way experienced injectors reason — pattern recognition, the pre-mortem, the pre-injection safety pause — and recognize when a patient is beyond your competence and must be referred *(Create/Evaluate).*
7. **Locate** this entire module within its proper limit: supervised, in-person practical training is the required next step before you inject a patient *(Understand).*

## 9.1 · From Assessment to Plan: Making the Reasoning Visible

A treatment plan is not a list of injection points. It is a *documented chain of reasoning* that connects what you observed to what you propose to do and why — a chain any reviewing clinician, and your future self at the two-week review, can follow. If a colleague read your plan without meeting the patient, they should be able to reconstruct your thinking.

Recall the pipeline from **Chapter 6**: you elicited goals, screened for red flags and body dysmorphic disorder, and performed a static-and-dynamic facial assessment that mapped findings to target muscles. Recall from **Chapter 7** that you have cleared the patient — no absolute contraindication, relative factors weighed, valid consent obtainable. The plan is where those two streams converge.

A defensible plan makes four things explicit, in order:

- **Goal, in the patient's words.** "Soften the frown so I look less angry" is a target you can plan toward and later measure. "Make me look better" is not — send it back to the consultation.
- **Findings that justify each target.** Every muscle you propose to treat should trace to a *dynamic* finding you documented — a movement that creates the line the patient dislikes. A muscle with no relevant finding has no business on the plan. This is also your guard against over-treatment: if you cannot name the finding, you cannot justify the toxin.
- **Baseline asymmetries and cautions, recorded *before* you treat.** The pre-existing higher brow, the mild lid heaviness at rest, the deep static line that toxin alone will not erase — documented now, these are honest expectation-setting; documented never, they become "did the injector cause this?" at review. (Chapter 6 built this habit; the capstone enforces it.)
- **The proposed pattern and its rationale**, area by area, in reasoning terms — *which* muscles, at *relative* dose, toward *what* balance — anchored to the elevator/depressor logic of **Chapters 4 and 5**.

> 💡 **The one-sentence test for every target.** For each muscle on your plan, finish this sentence out loud: *"I am treating this muscle because I observed ___, and softening it will ___, without unbalancing ___."* If you cannot complete all three blanks, the target is not ready — you are guessing, and guessing is how the frontalis gets over-treated and the brow drops.

The treatment-planning worksheet in the next section turns this reasoning into a repeatable form.

## 9.2 · The Treatment-Planning Worksheet

The worksheet is a thinking tool, not paperwork. Its columns force the reasoning of §9.1 to be visible and force *you* to justify each line before a needle is ever discussed. Fill it during the consult, refine it against the mirror with the patient, and keep it as the backbone of the treatment record (§9.6).

**Treatment-planning worksheet (asset M1-36)**

| Field | What you record | Why it earns its place |
|---|---|---|
| **Patient goal (verbatim)** | The result in the patient's own words | Anchors the plan to *their* aim, not your aesthetic; the yardstick at review |
| **Dynamic findings** | What each target muscle *does* on animation (frown, raise, squint) | A target with no finding is unjustified; the guard against treating "just because" |
| **Static findings** | Lines/asymmetries present at rest | Sets honest expectations — toxin softens movement, not etched lines |
| **Baseline asymmetry / cautions** | Pre-existing brow or lid differences, ptosis risk, prior response | Protects the patient and you; distinguishes pre-existing from treatment-caused |
| **Target muscles** | The specific muscles to treat, per area | The plan proper — each must trace to a dynamic finding |
| **Relative dose intent** | Conservative / moderate / fuller — *reasoned*, with ⚠ reference values deferred to monograph | Captures strategy (§9.3) without committing a fixed number on paper |
| **Elevator/depressor balance note** | How this pattern preserves brow position and expression | Forces full-face thinking; prevents the isolated over-treatment that drops brows |
| **Deliberately NOT treating** | Areas left alone this cycle, and why | Under-treatment is a *choice*, documented — the setup for the 2-week review |
| **Consent & photography** | Confirmed consent; standardized baseline photos taken | Medico-legal spine (Chapters 6–7) |
| **Plan rationale (summary)** | 2–3 sentences a reviewer could follow cold | The chain of reasoning, captured |

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm the worksheet fields align with the treatment-record and consent-documentation expectations Margie wants enforced for Ontario practice, and that "relative dose intent" (rather than a fixed unit field) is the preferred way to keep dosing conceptual on the planning form.

## 9.3 · Dosing *Strategy*: Reasoning, Not Recipes

This is the section most likely to be misread, so read it precisely. **This module does not teach you a dose to give a patient.** It teaches you how experienced injectors *reason* about dose — the variables that push a plan toward "more" or "less" — so that when you later train under supervision with current product monographs in front of you, the numbers make sense instead of being memorized blindly.

Three principles frame everything:

- **Units are product-specific.** From **Chapter 3**: a "unit" is a manufacturer-specific measure of biological activity. Any relative reasoning below is *within a single product*; it never converts across products. The safe default when unsure is *less*, then review.
- **You can always add; you cannot subtract.** Toxin is not reversible on demand. Under-treating and reviewing at two weeks (§9.7) is a *strategy*, not a failure — it is how careful injectors avoid the heavy brow, the frozen forehead, and the distressed patient.
- **The dose serves the goal, and the goal is usually "natural."** Most first-time patients want to look *rested*, not *expressionless*. A natural result generally means treating enough to soften movement while preserving some animation — which usually argues for the conservative end of a reasoned range, especially in the frontalis.

The variables that move a plan up or down are best held as a modifier table. Read it as *directional reasoning*, never as arithmetic.

**Dosing-modifier reasoning (asset M1-37)**

| Modifier | Direction of reasoning | Why (the mechanism, not a number) |
|---|---|---|
| **Muscle mass / bulk** | Bulkier, stronger muscle → toward higher relative dose | More motor units to quiet; a stronger frown or a heavy, mobile frontalis resists a light dose |
| **Sex (as a proxy for typical muscle mass & brow aesthetics)** | Men *on average* → toward higher relative dose and a flatter brow aim | Greater average muscle mass; the masculine brow is typically lower and straighter, so lateral frontalis strategy differs |
| **Prior response** | Faded fast / under-responded last cycle → consider adjusting upward; over-responded / brow dropped → downward | The patient's own history is the best predictor; secondary non-response is a separate Chapter 8 consideration |
| **"Natural" vs "frozen" goal** | Natural → toward conservative, movement-preserving; "frozen" request → *pause and counsel*, do not simply comply | The goal sets the target; a request to be immobile is an expectation conversation, not a dosing instruction |
| **Baseline brow position / ptosis risk** | Low brow, dermatochalasis, prior heaviness → toward caution, esp. frontalis | Frontalis is the sole brow *elevator*; over-quieting it drops the brow — the classic beginner harm (Chapter 8) |
| **Age / skin quality & static lines** | Deep static lines → set expectations, *not* higher dose | More toxin will not erase an etched line; over-dosing to chase it causes harm without benefit |

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** All *specific* unit ranges for frontalis, glabellar complex, and lateral canthal lines are to be supplied as cited educational reference from current product monographs and published upper-face consensus during the evidence pass — **not asserted here**. Confirm the sex/muscle-mass generalizations are framed as population tendencies with individual assessment overriding, and that the "natural vs frozen" row reads as a *counseling* trigger, not a dosing directive.

> 💡 **Full-face thinking, even in the upper face.** You never treat a muscle in isolation — you treat a *balance*. Quiet the glabellar depressors and the frontalis may lift the medial brow more; quiet the frontalis and the depressors win, dropping the brow; treat one lateral canthus harder than the other and you create asymmetry the patient will notice in every photo. Plan the *pattern*, then reason each point against the whole.

## 9.4 · When to Under-treat, and When to Refer

Two of the most important decisions in this chapter are decisions to do *less* — or to do *nothing*.

**Deliberate under-treatment** is the signature of a mature injector, not a timid one. You choose the conservative end when: the patient is a first-timer whose response you cannot predict; the goal is explicitly natural; the frontalis or brow position is at risk; or the anatomy is asymmetric and you would rather add precisely at review than chase symmetry with more toxin now. Under-treatment is not "getting it wrong" — it is *sequencing* the treatment across the injection and the two-week review, keeping every option open.

**Referral** is the other. Knowing your limits is a clinical skill, and this module has been explicit about its own limit throughout. Refer, defer, or escalate rather than proceed when:

- the anatomy, asymmetry, or request is beyond what you have been trained and supervised to manage;
- the patient screens positive for BDD or unrealistic expectations no counseling has resolved (**Chapter 6**);
- a relative contraindication tips the risk/benefit against treating today (**Chapter 7**);
- the plan would require areas outside your authorized scope or beyond this module's upper-face focus;
- anything about the encounter makes you uncertain you can proceed *safely* — uncertainty itself is a stop.

> 💡 **"When in doubt, don't" is a plan, too.** Declining or deferring is never a failure of the consultation — it is often the highest-skill outcome of it. The injector who refers the wrong patient has practised excellent medicine.

This connects directly to the pre-injection safety pause (§9.8) and to the module's closing point (§9.9): the judgment you are building here tells you *when* to act; supervised practical training is what qualifies you to act.

## 9.5 · Aftercare: The Rationale Behind Every Instruction

Patients follow aftercare advice they *understand* and ignore advice that sounds like superstition — so you must know which of your own instructions rest on evidence and which are convention or myth. The distinction is not academic: giving a patient a confident, mechanistic reason ("stay upright a few hours so the product settles where we placed it") earns adherence; repeating folklore erodes your credibility and, worse, can be flatly wrong.

The sheet below grades common instructions by the strength of their basis. Read the evidence-grade column as *how firmly you should assert this*, and note that several widely repeated rules are weakly supported or contested.

**Evidence-graded aftercare sheet (asset M1-39)**

| Instruction | Usual rationale given | Evidence status | How to counsel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stay upright for a few hours; avoid lying face-down** | Limit unwanted spread of product from placement site | Conventional / theoretical — commonly advised, limited high-quality evidence | Offer as sensible precaution, not iron law; avoid over-promising the mechanism ⚠ |
| **Avoid vigorous exercise / heat (e.g., sauna) for ~24 h** | Reduce blood flow that might increase spread or bruising | Conventional / mixed evidence | Reasonable, low-cost precaution; frame as "we err on the safe side" ⚠ |
| **Avoid rubbing/massaging the treated area** | Prevent mechanical spread to adjacent muscles (e.g., toward lid) | Plausible mechanism; widely advised | Advise gently; genuinely relevant near periorbital danger zones (Chapter 8) |
| **Expected minor bruising / small bumps / mild headache** | Injection-related, self-limited (Chapter 8) | Well recognized as common, self-limited | Normalize proactively so it isn't mistaken for a complication |
| **"Frowning/animating the area helps the toxin work"** | Belief that muscle activity increases uptake | Weak / contested — often stated as fact | Do **not** present as established; flag as unproven ⚠ |
| **Avoid other facial treatments/manipulation for a short window** | Minimize spread and infection risk | Conventional | Reasonable, individualized to the treatment |
| **When to call the clinic** | Escalation for asymmetry, lid heaviness, visual change, spreading redness | Safety-critical | The most important item — give explicit red flags and a contact route (Chapter 8) |

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Each row's *evidence status* must be checked against current literature before publication — several standard aftercare rules (upright posture duration, exercise/heat timing, "animate the area") are convention or contested rather than well-evidenced, and must not be presented as proven. Confirm the specific time windows Margie endorses, and that the "when to call" red-flag list matches the Chapter 8 escalation criteria exactly.

> 💡 **Aftercare's real job is the safety net.** The upright-posture and no-rubbing advice is minor; the *"here is exactly what to watch for and how to reach us"* instruction is what protects the patient. Every aftercare conversation should end there, not begin there.

## 9.6 · The Treatment Record: Documenting to a Medico-legal Standard

If it isn't written down, it didn't happen — and in an elective procedure on a well patient, the record is your single best protection and the patient's single best continuity of care. The two-week review, any complication work-up (Chapter 8), and any future cycle all depend on a record precise enough to *reproduce or adjust* exactly what you did.

A defensible treatment record captures, at minimum:

- **Product and manufacturer** — the specific Health Canada–approved product used (units are product-specific; Chapter 3).
- **Lot number and expiry**, and **reconstitution details** (diluent, volume, resulting concentration) — traceability if a product issue arises, and the basis for reproducibility.
- **Injection map** — a facial diagram marking each point, the muscle, and the units delivered *per point*, so the total and the distribution are both recoverable.
- **Total units per area and overall.**
- **Consent confirmation** and **standardized baseline photographs** (Chapters 6–7).
- **Baseline asymmetries/cautions** noted pre-treatment (from the worksheet, §9.2).
- **Aftercare given** and the red-flag/contact instructions provided (§9.5).
- **Authorizing mechanism** — the order or medical directive under which you administered (Chapter 1), and the prescriber.
- **Date, time, injector, and any immediate events.**

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm this record set meets CNO documentation standards and Ontario medico-legal expectations for cosmetic injectables, and that recording units-per-point on an injection map (not merely a total) is the standard Margie wants taught. Any regulatory specifics are subject to regulatory sign-off.

> 💡 **The injection map is not decoration.** A record that says "20 units, glabella" cannot tell you, at review, *where* the asymmetry came from or *how* to correct it. A map that says *which point, which muscle, how many units* turns the two-week review from guesswork into a precise adjustment.

The planning worksheet (§9.2), the aftercare sheet (§9.5), and this record are one continuous document — plan, do, instruct, record — not three forms.

## 9.7 · The Two-Week Review and the Touch-up Decision

You dosed conservatively (§9.3) on purpose, which means the treatment is not finished at the injection — it is finished at the **two-week review**, when the toxin has reached peak effect (the onset → peak timeline from **Chapter 2**). Reviewing before peak invites the classic error: adding more to a result that had not yet fully arrived, then watching the patient over-treat as both doses mature.

The review is a structured re-assessment, not a courtesy call:

- **Re-photograph** with the same standardized set and **compare to baseline** — objective evidence, not memory.
- **Re-assess dynamically**: is residual movement *bothering the patient*, or only visible to you? The patient's goal (§9.1) is the yardstick, not perfection.
- **Check balance**: any asymmetry, brow-position change, or heaviness — and is it settling, worsening, or a complication needing the Chapter 8 pathway?
- **Decide**, against the plan's stated intent.

The touch-up-versus-leave-alone decision is where over-treatment is either prevented or committed:

- **Touch up** when there is a *specific, patient-relevant* residual finding that a small, targeted addition will address without unbalancing the face — and you can name the point and the muscle.
- **Leave alone** when the result meets the patient's goal, or when the residual movement is minor, or when adding toxin would risk the balance (e.g., chasing symmetry into a dropped brow). "It could be a touch stronger" is not, by itself, a reason to inject.
- **Escalate/refer** if the review reveals a complication beyond supportive management (Chapter 8).

> 💡 **Over-treatment is rarely one big mistake — it's a series of small "just a little mores."** Each touch-up feels reasonable in isolation; the frozen forehead is their sum. The discipline is to weigh every addition against the *patient's goal and the facial balance*, not against an abstract ideal of "smoother."

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Confirm the two-week (peak-effect) review window and the touch-up framing against current consensus/monograph guidance; timing and touch-up conventions are citation-sensitive.

## 9.8 · How Experts Reason: Pattern, Pre-mortem, and the Pause

The difference between a novice and an expert injector is less in the hands than in the *thinking* that precedes the hands. Three habits, learnable now, define it.

**Pattern recognition.** Experts don't reason every case from scratch — they recognize *types* ("first-timer, natural goal, strong glabella, at-risk brow") and retrieve a reasoned approach, then adjust for the individual. You build these patterns by doing the cases in this module deliberately, not by memorizing doses. Early on, reason slowly and explicitly (the worksheet forces this); the patterns come with supervised repetition.

**The pre-mortem.** Before you finalize a plan, imagine it is two weeks later and the result is *bad*. Ask: *what went wrong?* Brow dropped because the frontalis was over-quieted? Asymmetry because one side got more? Patient distressed because "natural" wasn't clarified and they feel frozen? A deep static line the toxin was never going to fix, unaddressed in consent? The pre-mortem surfaces the specific, foreseeable failure of *this* plan while you can still change it — and most of what it surfaces traces back to a step skipped in Chapters 6–8.

**The pre-injection safety pause.** Immediately before injecting — a deliberate, every-single-time stop, the aesthetic sibling of the surgical time-out:

- **Right patient, right authority?** Correct patient; valid order/directive whose conditions they meet (Chapter 1); consent confirmed and current (Chapter 7).
- **Right product, right preparation?** Correct product, in date, reconstituted correctly, dose reasoned and documented (Chapter 3, §9.3).
- **Right plan, right map?** Targets match the assessment; asymmetries and cautions noted; injection map ready (§9.2, §9.6).
- **Any reason to stop?** New information, new hesitation, anything unresolved — *the pause is permission to not proceed.*

> 💡 **The pause is worth more than any single point of technique.** A perfectly placed injection into the wrong plan, the wrong patient, or an unclarified expectation is still a bad outcome. The pause is the last, cheapest, most powerful place to catch all three.

## Case Study 3 — Treatment Planning

> **Assessment note.** This case carries assessment load for Objectives 1–4. Work it as reasoning: build the plan, don't retrieve a dose.

**Presentation.** *Dara,* 38, first-ever neuromodulator patient, cleared in consultation (no contraindications; consent obtainable; realistic, engaged). Her goal, verbatim: *"I look angry even when I'm not — I want to soften that, but I'm scared of looking frozen or fake. I still want to move my face."* On dynamic assessment: a strong glabellar frown (corrugators drawing the brows together, procerus creating a horizontal root crease); moderate frontalis activity raising the forehead with fine horizontal lines on animation; minimal lateral canthal activity on full smile. At rest: brows symmetric, no ptosis, only faint static glabellar shadowing. No prior treatment history to draw on.

**Reasoning to a plan.**

- **Goal → strategy.** "Soften but stay natural, still want to move" is an explicit *natural* goal from a first-timer with no known response. Both facts point the same way: **conservative, movement-preserving dosing**, sequenced across treatment and a two-week review (§9.3, §9.7). This is textbook *deliberate under-treatment* (§9.4).
- **Glabella (the primary complaint).** The "angry" look traces directly to the glabellar complex — the strong frown is the documented dynamic finding that justifies the target. Treat the glabellar depressors to soften the frown; this is the core of the plan. *One-sentence test:* treating the corrugators/procerus because the strong frown creates the "angry" look, softening it addresses her primary goal, without dropping the brow if dose stays reasoned.
- **Frontalis (treat, but cautiously — full-face thinking).** Two reasons to be careful. First, the frontalis is the sole brow *elevator* (Chapters 4–5): over-quieting it, especially alongside glabellar treatment, risks brow heaviness. Second, her goal is natural — she wants to keep moving. A light, balanced approach to the upper frontalis that preserves some lift and expression fits both the anatomy and the goal. Under-treat and review.
- **Lateral canthal lines.** Minimal dynamic finding → **deliberately not treating this cycle** (documented as a choice, §9.2). No finding, no target — treating them would be chasing an area she didn't raise and that doesn't animate.
- **Static shadowing.** Faint and static → set expectations in consent (§9.5): toxin softens the *movement*; the faint resting shadow may persist. Do not raise dose to chase it.
- **Balance note.** Softening glabellar depressors while lightly treating frontalis keeps the elevator/depressor balance intact and the brow where it started — the pre-mortem's chief worry ("dropped brow") is thereby designed out.
- **Aftercare & record.** Standard aftercare with rationale (§9.5), red flags and contact route emphasized; full record with injection map and units-per-point (§9.6); two-week review booked *now* as part of the plan, not as damage control.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** No specific unit values are stated in this plan by design. If worked reference ranges are added for teaching, they must come from current monographs/consensus and be flagged as educational reference, not directives.

**Teaching point.** Notice that every target traces to a documented finding, every *non*-target is a recorded choice, and the whole plan is engineered around a natural goal and an unknown first-time response — conservative, balanced, and reviewed. That is a complete plan, and no memorized dose was required to build it.

## Case Study 5 — Capstone

> **Assessment note.** The integrative case. It carries the heaviest assessment load — Objectives 1–7 — and rewards end-to-end reasoning from consult to aftercare, including the discipline to *not* treat parts of what's asked.

**Presentation.** *Marcus,* 52, returning for a second opinion. Requests: *"I want my forehead and frown completely smooth — no lines at all — and get rid of these too,"* gesturing at deep static forehead lines and at crow's-feet. History: one prior treatment elsewhere ~4 months ago that "dropped my eyebrows and made my forehead heavy for weeks — I hated it." On assessment: heavy, strong frontalis with a naturally **low, flat brow** and mild dermatochalasis (upper-lid skin laxity); very strong glabellar frown; deep *static* horizontal forehead lines present at rest; active lateral canthal lines on smile. Mentions, in passing, that his prior injector "just kept adding more when it wasn't smooth enough." Takes no relevant medications; no absolute contraindication surfaces on history.

**End-to-end reasoning.**

1. **Consultation & expectations (Chapter 6, §9.1).** The request — *"completely smooth, no lines at all"* — is a **"frozen" goal**, and the modifier table says to *pause and counsel*, not comply (§9.3). Two expectation problems must be resolved before any plan: (a) the *deep static* forehead lines will **not** fully erase with toxin — that is an etched-line limit, not a dosing shortfall (over-dosing to chase them is exactly the harm to avoid); and (b) "no movement at all" in a 52-year-old man with a low brow is a recipe for the very heaviness he already hated. This is an expectation conversation first.
2. **Screen the request and the history (Chapters 6–7).** His account of the prior injector "just kept adding more" is a red flag for the **over-treatment cascade** (§9.7) — and a cue to check *his* expectations, not just repeat the pattern. His own prior *bad response (brow drop, heaviness)* is the single best predictor here (§9.3, "prior response"): it argues strongly toward **caution and downward** dosing in the frontalis, whatever the average-male reasoning might otherwise suggest. Individual history overrides the population tendency.
3. **The frontalis problem (Chapters 4–5, §9.3–9.4).** Low, flat brow + dermatochalasis + a documented prior brow drop = **high-risk frontalis, at-risk brow.** The reasoning points to treating the frontalis *lightly and high*, preserving lift — or under-treating it markedly and reviewing — precisely to protect brow position. Chasing "completely smooth" here would reproduce his complication.
4. **Glabella & lateral canthal lines.** Strong glabellar frown is a well-justified target (clear dynamic finding). Active lateral canthal lines are a legitimate target *if* within your trained, authorized scope — and note from Chapter 5 the lateral-canthus cautions (stay lateral, avoid smile asymmetry). If this is beyond what you've been supervised to do, that area is a **referral/defer**, not an improvisation (§9.4).
5. **Pre-mortem (§9.8).** Imagine two weeks out and it's *bad*: brow dropped again (frontalis over-treated) → design in caution and under-treatment; patient furious the forehead "still has lines" (static lines, expectation not set) → fix in consent now; asymmetric crow's-feet (lateral area beyond skill) → refer that component. Every foreseeable failure traces to a step we can take *now*.
6. **Plan, conservatively and honestly.** Treat the glabellar complex (clear finding). Treat the frontalis *lightly*, protecting the brow, with an explicit plan to **review and add at two weeks if needed** rather than max out today. Address lateral canthal lines only within scope, else refer. **Document** the deep static lines as a discussed limitation the patient understands; **do not** raise dose to chase them.
7. **Consent (Chapter 7).** Explicit, documented, no-guarantee framing: the static lines will *soften, not vanish*; full immobility is not the goal and would risk the heaviness he disliked; realistic outcome described in his own terms.
8. **Aftercare & record (§9.5–9.6).** Rationale-based aftercare; emphasized red flags given his history of heaviness (what to watch for, how to reach the clinic); complete record with injection map and units-per-point; **two-week review booked** as the mechanism to add precisely rather than over-treat now.
9. **Know the edge (§9.4, §9.9).** If any element — the at-risk brow, the lateral area, the "frozen" demand — sits beyond your supervised competence, the correct capstone answer is to **defer or refer**, not to prove you can. That judgment *is* the competency this module builds.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** All dose reasoning is directional. Any teaching ranges added for frontalis/glabella/lateral canthal lines must be sourced to monographs/consensus and flagged as educational reference. Confirm the "static lines won't erase" and "prior brow drop predicts caution" framings match Margie's clinical guidance.

**Teaching point.** The capstone answer is *not* the smoothest forehead. It is the plan that (a) resets an unsafe expectation, (b) lets the patient's own history overrule an average, (c) protects a vulnerable brow by under-treating and reviewing, (d) refuses to chase static lines with toxin, and (e) refers what exceeds competence. Integration means knowing which parts of the request to *decline*.

## OSCE-Style Station

> **Format.** A simulated, rubric-scored encounter assessing **judgment and communication — not needle skill.** No injection is performed or simulated on a person. The candidate talks and reasons through a standardized "patient" (a peer or actor) and a facial-assessment prompt (photo set or manikin), while an assessor scores against the rubric. Runs ~15–20 minutes. This station anchors the Module 1 OSCE and is completed before supervised practical training, not in place of it.

**The scenario (given to the candidate).** A standardized patient presents for upper-face neuromodulator treatment with a stated goal. The candidate must move through six phases in order: **consultation → facial assessment → plan with rationale → consent & photography → pre-injection safety pause → complication-awareness Q&A.** The assessor may inject a curveball (an unrealistic "make me frozen" request, a passing mention of a relative contraindication, a pre-existing asymmetry the candidate must catch).

**Scoring rubric (asset M1-38 supports the flow).** Each domain scored 0 = not done / unsafe · 1 = partial · 2 = competent · 3 = fluent. A score of 0 on any *safety-critical* domain (marked ⚑) is an automatic station fail regardless of total.

| # | Domain | What competent (2–3) looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Consultation & goal elicitation** | Open questions; captures goal in patient's words; probes real motivation |
| 2 | **Red-flag / expectation screen** ⚑ | Actively screens BDD/unrealistic expectations; handles a "frozen" or unrealistic request by counseling, not complying |
| 3 | **Structured facial assessment** | Static *and* dynamic; identifies target muscles; catches baseline asymmetry/ptosis risk |
| 4 | **Plan with rationale** | Each target traces to a finding; states relative-dose *intent* conservatively; notes elevator/depressor balance; names what is *not* treated and why |
| 5 | **Authorization check** ⚑ | Confirms valid order/medical directive whose conditions the patient meets (Chapter 1) |

## Clinical Pearls

- **A plan is a chain of reasoning, not a list of points.** If a colleague couldn't reconstruct your thinking from the record, the plan isn't finished — and neither is the documentation.
- **You can add; you can't subtract.** Under-treatment plus a two-week review is a strategy that keeps every option open. Over-treatment closes them.
- **Every target must pass the one-sentence test.** Observed ___ , softening it will ___ , without unbalancing ___ . No finding, no toxin.
- **The most important aftercare line is "here's what to watch for and how to reach us."** The upright-posture advice is minor; the safety net is everything.
- **The pre-mortem finds tomorrow's complication while you can still prevent it** — and most of what it finds traces to a step skipped earlier in the module.
- **"When in doubt, don't" is a high-skill outcome.** Referring the wrong patient is excellent medicine.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Treating a muscle with no documented dynamic finding** — the root of over-treatment. If you can't name the finding, don't treat the muscle.
- **Reviewing before peak effect (~two weeks) and adding more**, then watching both doses mature into an over-treated result.
- **Chasing deep static lines with more toxin.** Etched lines are an expectation conversation, not a dosing problem — piling on dose adds harm without benefit.
- **Complying with a "make me frozen" request instead of counseling it.** The goal drives the plan; an immobility demand is a red flag to address, not an instruction to follow.
- **Over-quieting the frontalis and dropping the brow** — forgetting it is the sole brow elevator, and that glabellar treatment shifts the balance too.
- **Documenting a total ("20 units, glabella") instead of an injection map with units-per-point** — leaving the two-week review to guesswork.
- **Skipping the pre-injection pause because the plan "looks obvious."** The obvious plans are where wrong-patient and unclarified-expectation errors hide.

## Summary

This capstone assembled the module. A **treatment plan** is a documented chain of reasoning from goal to targets, each target traceable to a dynamic finding and each non-target a recorded choice — captured on a **planning worksheet** (M1-36). **Dosing is taught as strategy, not recipe**: units are product-specific, you can add but not subtract, and the natural goal most patients want argues for conservative, movement-preserving reasoning tailored by muscle bulk, sex, prior response, and goal (M1-37) — with all specific values deferred to monographs and the evidence pass. **Deliberate under-treatment** and **referral** are high-skill decisions, not failures. **Aftercare** instructions each carry a rationale, and several familiar rules are convention or myth, not evidence (M1-39); the safety-net "what to watch for and how to reach us" matters most. The **treatment record** — product, lot, reconstitution, injection map with units-per-point, consent, photos, authorizing mechanism — is documented to a medico-legal standard. The **two-week (peak-effect) review** governs the touch-up-versus-leave decision, the last defence against the over-treatment cascade. Experts reason by **pattern recognition, the pre-mortem, and the pre-injection safety pause**, and they **know their limits**. Case Studies 3 and 5 and the OSCE station integrate all of it. And the module's final, non-negotiable point: what you have earned here is *judgment* — **supervised, in-person practical training is the required next step before you inject a patient.**
