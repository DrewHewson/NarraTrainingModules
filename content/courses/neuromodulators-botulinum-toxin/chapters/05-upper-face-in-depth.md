## 5.1 · Injection Principles

Before we look at any single area, we build the four ideas that govern *all three*. Every area-specific decision later in the chapter is really just one of these four principles applied to a particular muscle. Learn them as a system and the areas will feel like variations on a theme rather than three lists to memorize.

### 5.1.1 · Depth: matching the plane to the muscle

The single question behind depth is: **where does the muscle I want to weaken actually live, and what is directly beneath it that I must not weaken?**

Upper-face muscles of facial expression sit in a **superficial plane** — they are, broadly, muscles that move skin, so they lie close to it. But "superficial" is relative and it is not uniform across the face:

- The **frontalis** is a broad, flat sheet lying just under the skin of the forehead, over the frontal bone. Beneath it is bone, not another muscle you can harm — the danger in the forehead is not *what's underneath* but *where the frontalis connects to the brow elevators*, which we return to below.
- The **glabellar complex** (corrugators, procerus, depressor supercilii) is more three-dimensional. The corrugator in particular runs from a deep bony origin near the medial brow and travels *upward and outward*, becoming more superficial as it goes. So the same muscle may be addressed at different depths along its length — a key reason the glabella is taught as the most technically demanding of the three.
- The **orbicularis oculi**, responsible for lateral canthal lines, is extremely **superficial and thin** — essentially just under the skin at the temple and lateral eye. This is why crow's-feet are treated with the *shallowest* placement of the three areas.

> 💡 **The expert's depth question is never "how deep is standard?" — it is "what is directly under my needle tip, and do I want it affected?"** Depth is a safety decision before it is a technique decision. Placing product too deep near the orbital rim, or too medial and deep in the forehead, is how the drug reaches muscles you never intended to treat.

The reasoning connects directly to **Chapter 4**: because these are thin, superficial muscles, product placed too deep doesn't just "miss" — it pools in a plane where it can **diffuse toward structures below**, most dangerously the **levator palpebrae superioris** (the eyelid elevator) deep behind the orbital septum. You cannot see that muscle and you are not treating it, but depth and placement determine whether your product drifts toward it.

### 5.1.2 · Dose as three linked concepts

Beginners think of dose as one number ("how many units for the forehead?"). Experts think of it as three linked concepts, each answering a different question:

| Concept | The question it answers | What drives it |
|---|---|---|
| **Per-point dose** | How much goes into *this* single injection site? | Muscle bulk at that point; proximity to a danger zone; desired local effect |
| **Per-muscle (per-area) dose** | How much total does *this muscle/area* need to soften its movement? | Muscle strength/mass; how much movement the patient wants preserved; sex and baseline muscle bulk |
| **Per-session total** | How much toxin does the patient receive across *all* areas today? | Sum of areas treated; conservative-first philosophy; patient's history and goals |

These are not independent. Lowering a per-point dose near a danger zone but keeping the per-muscle target constant means **spreading the same total across more, smaller points** — trading concentration for a wider, gentler field. Raising the per-session total by adding an area changes the risk conversation even if each area is individually conservative. The expert holds all three in view at once.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Typical *ranges* for per-point, per-muscle, and per-session dosing for each area are compiled in the dosing table (Table 5-A / asset **M1-33**). Every value there is cited educational reference drawn from published consensus and product monographs, **product-specific and non-interchangeable** (recall Chapter 3), and must be verified before publication. None is a directive for any individual patient; real dosing is individualized under medical supervision.

A governing philosophy sits above all three numbers, and it is worth stating plainly because it is the safest habit in this chapter:

> 💡 **Dose conservatively, then review.** It is always easier to *add* at the two-week review than to wait out an over-treatment. Under-treating a first-time patient and topping up is a feature of expert practice, not a failure of it. We formalize the two-week review in **Chapter 9**.

### 5.1.3 · The field of diffusion: dilution, volume, and placement

When you inject, the toxin does not stay at a mathematical point. It spreads outward from the injection site into a roughly spherical **field of diffusion** (sometimes called the field of effect or halo). Everything within that field is affected; everything outside it is spared. **Controlling the size and position of that field is the core safety skill of injecting** — and it is governed by three levers you control:

1. **Dilution (concentration).** The same number of units reconstituted in *more* diluent produces a *larger* volume at a *lower* concentration. Larger volume generally spreads farther. This is the direct link back to **Chapter 3**: your reconstitution choice is not just bookkeeping — it pre-sets how far your product will travel. A higher dilution (more volume) can be useful for a broad, even muscle like the frontalis but is *hazardous* near the orbital rim where you want a tight, contained field.
2. **Volume injected per point.** Even at a fixed concentration, pushing a larger bolus into one spot inflates the field. Near a danger zone, experts favour *smaller* volumes at *more* points to keep each field small and predictable.
3. **Placement (site and depth).** Where the centre of the field sits determines what its edges reach. A field of a given size is safe in the mid-forehead and dangerous 1 cm from the orbital rim — same field, different neighbourhood.

> 💡 **Diffusion is not the enemy — *uncontrolled* diffusion is.** A little spread is how a few points soften a whole muscle. The skill is keeping the *edge* of the field away from structures you must not weaken. When in doubt near a danger zone: lower volume, lower dilution (more concentrated / smaller volume), more points, stay farther from the rim.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Any specific claim about a product's relative diffusion characteristics ("field of effect" comparisons between products) must be verified against current evidence and monographs. Comparative-diffusion claims between BoNT-A products are contested in the literature and must not be asserted unverified.

### 5.1.4 · Spacing, danger zones, and unintended spread

**Spacing** is diffusion applied across multiple points. Points placed too close together create *overlapping* fields — a locally excessive dose and an unpredictable combined effect. Points placed too far apart leave untreated muscle between them, producing patchy, uneven softening. Correct spacing is simply choosing an interval where adjacent fields *meet without heavily overlapping*.

The **danger zones** of the upper face are the regions where an escaping field reaches a muscle you never intended to treat. Three matter most, and each maps to a specific complication you must be able to explain:

| Danger zone (conceptual) | What lives there that you must not weaken | The complication if the field reaches it |
|---|---|---|
| **Deep/central mid-orbit, behind the septum** | **Levator palpebrae superioris** (eyelid elevator) | **Eyelid ptosis** (a drooping upper lid) |
| **Lower/lateral forehead near the brow** | The brow-elevating portion of **frontalis** | **Brow ptosis / heaviness** (if too much/too low) or a peaked **"Spock" brow** (if unevenly treated) |
| **Anterior/inferior to the lateral canthus, over the zygoma** | **Zygomaticus major** (a smile elevator) | **Smile asymmetry / lip drop** on that side |

> ⚠ **The danger zones are the reason this chapter is conceptual and hands-on is supervised.** You can memorize where the levator lives, but *keeping a diffusion field away from it in a real face* is a motor and spatial skill acquired under a proctor. Reading this chapter makes you able to explain the danger; it does not make you safe to inject near it. Do not conflate the two.

The single most important danger-zone rule in the upper face is the one that prevents the most feared complication:

> 💡 **The levator rule.** Eyelid ptosis comes from product diffusing through the orbital septum to the levator. The classic teaching to avoid it — inject the glabellar/medial-brow region **conservatively, not too deep, and not too low / too close to the orbital rim**, keeping fields small and contained — is a diffusion-control rule, not a "mark this spot" rule. We revisit ptosis mechanism and management in depth in **Chapter 8**.

### 5.1.5 · Asepsis and bruising minimization

Two final principles apply to every area and belong to your existing nursing competence, now applied in an aesthetic context:

- **Asepsis.** These are injections into a clean, elective, well patient. Standard aseptic technique — hand hygiene, skin cleansing per your clinic's protocol, single-patient handling of reconstituted product (Chapter 3), no double-dipping — is non-negotiable. Infection in an elective cosmetic patient is both a clinical harm and a professional-accountability event.
- **Bruising minimization.** The upper face is vascular, especially the periorbital region and the glabella. The *reasoning* for minimizing bruising includes: avoiding visible superficial vessels, using good lighting, minimizing needle passes, applying gentle post-injection pressure, and screening in advance for bleeding risk and agents that increase it (anticoagulants, some supplements — detailed in **Chapter 7**). A bruise is usually minor, but in the periorbital area it is visible, and setting the expectation *before* treatment is part of consent, not an afterthought.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Specific pre-treatment bruising-risk guidance (e.g., which supplements or agents to discuss, and any advice about their peri-procedural management) must be verified and cross-referenced with Chapter 7; nursing must never counsel a patient to alter prescribed medication without prescriber involvement.

## 5.2 · Frontalis

**Why we treat it.** The frontalis is the only true *elevator* of the brow. When it contracts to raise the brows and express surprise, it throws the forehead into **horizontal lines**. Softening the frontalis reduces those horizontal forehead lines. But — and this is the whole difficulty of the forehead — because the frontalis is *also* what holds the brows up, weakening it too much or too low lets the brows fall.

**How the expert thinks.** The forehead is a study in **balance, not maximal effect**. The expert is not trying to abolish frontalis movement; they are trying to soften the lines *while preserving enough elevation to keep the brows up and the eyes open-looking.* This is the elevator/depressor logic from **Chapter 4** made real: the frontalis (elevator) is in a tug-of-war with the glabellar and orbicularis depressors, and the injector's job is to *tune* that balance, not to win it for one side.

Practically, three ideas dominate expert forehead reasoning:

- **Treat the forehead in relation to the brow depressors, not in isolation.** Over-relaxing the frontalis while leaving the depressors strong lets the depressors "win," dragging the brow down. This is why the forehead and glabella are so often reasoned about *together*.
- **Stay high enough.** Product placed too low on the forehead — too close to the brow — weakens exactly the fibres holding the brow up. Keeping the treated field in the **upper/mid forehead**, a safe margin above the brow, is the central bruise-free way to avoid brow heaviness.
- **Match the pattern to the patient's forehead.** Forehead height, hairline position, and how the lines actually fall vary enormously. A broad, tall forehead and a short, low one are not the same problem. (The *how many points and where* is precisely the kind of individualized, hands-on judgment finalized under supervision — not a fixed map.)

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Typical frontalis per-point / per-area dose ranges and the conventional "keep above the brow by a margin" teaching are compiled in Table 5-A (**M1-33**) as cited reference and must be verified. The safe margin above the brow is described in the literature in general terms; do not publish a specific measurement as a directive.

**When.** Frontalis treatment suits a patient bothered by **dynamic horizontal forehead lines** — lines that appear or deepen on raising the brows — who has adequate brow position and no dependence on frontalis over-activity to hold up a heavy or hooded brow.

**When NOT.** This is where judgment protects the patient:

- **Patients who unconsciously use their frontalis to hold up a heavy or hooded brow** (often older patients, or those with pre-existing brow ptosis / dermatochalasis). Weakening the frontalis in these patients removes their compensation and can produce a **heavy, hooded, tired look** — the opposite of what they wanted. Recognizing this at *assessment* (Chapter 6) is what prevents it.
- **Frontalis in isolation when the glabella is strong**, unless you have specifically reasoned through the elevator/depressor balance — treating the forehead alone can drop the brow.
- **Very low foreheads or low-set brows**, where the safe zone above the brow is small.

**What goes wrong.**

- **Brow ptosis / heaviness** — from over-treating, or treating too low, so the elevator can't hold the brow. Presents as heavy, low brows and a tired appearance.
- **"Spock" / quizzical peaked brow** — from treating the central forehead while leaving the lateral fibres active, so the outer brow peaks up. An *uneven* softening problem.
- **A frozen, expressionless forehead** — from over-treating for line abolition rather than line softening, sacrificing natural movement.

**How it's managed.** Prevention is the real management: conservative dosing, staying high, treating in balance with the depressors, and a **two-week review** to assess and top up. When a "Spock" brow appears, a small, carefully placed dose into the over-active lateral fibres can rebalance it (a correction performed under supervision, reasoned through, not improvised). True brow ptosis from frontalis over-treatment largely has to be *waited out* as the effect fades — which is exactly why conservative first-dosing matters so much. Full complication management is **Chapter 8**.

## 5.3 · Glabellar Complex

**Why we treat it.** The glabellar complex — the **corrugator supercilii**, **procerus**, and **depressor supercilii** — pulls the brows **down and together**, producing the vertical "**11**" lines between the brows and the horizontal crease at the root of the nose. These are the frown lines that read as anger, worry, or fatigue, and glabellar treatment is the original and most-requested cosmetic BoNT-A indication.

**How the expert thinks.** The glabella is the **highest-stakes upper-face area for the most feared complication — eyelid ptosis** — because the corrugators sit close to the orbital rim and the levator lies just deep and lateral to their medial fibres. So the expert's dominant thought here is *containment*: keep the diffusion field **small, contained, and away from the orbital septum.** Everything about glabellar reasoning bends toward not letting product reach the levator.

- **Depth varies along the muscle.** The corrugator originates deep and medially near the bone and runs superficially up and out toward the mid-brow. The expert appreciates that the *medial* corrugator sits deeper (near the origin) while the muscle becomes more superficial laterally — so a single "depth" for the glabella is a beginner's oversimplification.
- **Respect the levator's neighbourhood.** The classic ptosis-avoidance reasoning — don't inject **too low, too deep, too medial-and-inferior near the rim**, and keep volume/field small — is *all diffusion control* aimed at the septum. This is the levator rule from §5.1.4 in its most important application.
- **Balance with the frontalis.** Because the glabellar muscles are brow *depressors*, treating them lets the frontalis elevator "win" slightly and can give a pleasing, subtle brow lift — but over-treating the glabella while ignoring the forehead can also contribute to an unnatural arch. Balance, again.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Glabellar per-point and per-muscle dose ranges (corrugator, procerus, depressor supercilii) in Table 5-A (**M1-33**) are cited educational reference and must be verified against consensus and monographs. The specific anatomical "avoid" zones described here are conceptual; do not publish measured injection coordinates.

**When.** Suitable for a patient with **dynamic glabellar frown lines** — the "11s" that deepen on frowning — with acceptable brow and lid position and no contraindication (Chapter 7). It is often the anchor of a first, conservative upper-face treatment.

**When NOT.**

- **Pre-existing eyelid ptosis or poor levator function** — the margin for error near the levator is already reduced; this is a defer-and-reassess situation, and possibly a decline (Chapter 7).
- **Deep *static* glabellar lines etched at rest** — the patient must understand that BoNT-A softens the *dynamic* component; a line fully etched into the skin will improve but may not disappear, and over-dosing to chase it invites complications rather than better results. (Set this expectation at consultation — Chapter 6.)
- **Unrealistic expectations or a screening red flag** (e.g., BDD features) — a scope-and-ethics decision, not a technical one.

**What goes wrong.**

- **Eyelid ptosis** — the signature glabellar complication, from product diffusing to the levator. Typically appears days after treatment, and is the reason for every containment habit above.
- **Incomplete correction / persistent frowning** — from under-dosing a strong corrugator, or missing part of the muscle; corrected conservatively at review.
- **Brow-shape distortion** — from unbalanced treatment relative to the frontalis.
- **Bruising** — the glabella is vascular; visible bruising here is common if vessels aren't respected.

**How it's managed.** For **eyelid ptosis**, management is supportive and reassuring: it is **temporary** (the levator recovers as the effect fades), and a cited pharmacologic option — **apraclonidine ophthalmic drops**, which stimulate Müller's muscle to lift the lid a few millimetres — can be offered as a bridge while it resolves. Recognition, differentiation from brow ptosis, and management are covered fully in **Chapter 8**; the key point *here* is that the best management is the containment reasoning that prevents it.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** The use, availability, prescription status, and dosing of **apraclonidine** for toxin-related lid ptosis must be verified against current product information and Ontario prescribing rules before publication. It is named here only as a *cited option*; whether and how it is used is a prescriber decision.

## 5.4 · Lateral Canthal Lines (Crow's Feet)

**Why we treat it.** The **lateral canthal lines** — "crow's feet" — radiate from the outer corner of the eye when a person smiles or squints. They are produced by the **lateral fibres of the orbicularis oculi**, the sphincter-like muscle that closes the eye. Softening those lateral fibres reduces the fan of lines at the outer eye.

**How the expert thinks.** Two ideas dominate, and both are about *staying out of trouble that is very close by*:

- **This is the most superficial area — inject shallow.** The orbicularis at the temple is a thin sheet right under the skin. The reasoning for a very superficial placement is twofold: it's where the muscle is, *and* going deep here risks vessels (bruising is common and visible) and lets product travel where you don't want it.
- **Stay lateral, and respect the zone below.** The great danger of crow's-feet treatment is product diffusing **anteriorly and inferiorly toward the zygomaticus major**, a smile elevator sitting over the cheekbone. If the field reaches it, that side of the smile weakens — **smile asymmetry or a lip drop.** So the expert keeps the treated field **lateral and high enough**, a safe margin from the zygomatic arch and the mid-cheek, with small, superficial, contained fields. There is also an inferior/orbital consideration: staying an appropriate distance outside the bony orbital rim to avoid affecting the muscles that move the eye.

> 💡 **"Superficial, lateral, small."** The whole safety logic of crow's-feet compresses to three words: keep it **superficial** (it's a thin muscle and vascular), keep it **lateral** (away from zygomaticus and the mid-cheek), and keep fields **small** (contained, well-spaced, low volume). Every crow's-feet complication is a violation of one of these three.

> ⚠ **Verify before publication (Margie / evidence pass).** Lateral canthal per-point and per-area dose ranges, the number of points, and the conventional safe-margin distances from the orbital rim and zygoma (Table 5-A / **M1-33**) are cited educational reference and must be verified. Do not publish specific distances or coordinates as directives.

**When.** Suitable for **dynamic lateral canthal lines** that appear on smiling or squinting, in a patient who understands that lines etched at rest will soften but may persist, and who has no contraindication.

**When NOT.**

- **Prominent lower-lid festoons, malar bags, or poor lower-lid tone / snap** — weakening the lower orbicularis can worsen lower-lid function and puffiness. This is an assessment-driven "when not," recognized before you treat.
- **Patients whose smile aesthetics depend heavily on the treated fibres**, where the risk of asymmetry outweighs the benefit.
- **Very vascular or thin, easily-bruising periorbital skin** without a frank conversation about bruising as an expected possibility.

**What goes wrong.**

- **Smile asymmetry / lip drop** — from inferior/anterior diffusion to zygomaticus major. The signature crow's-feet complication.
- **Worsened lower-lid puffiness or a "hollow"/lower-lid weakness** — from treating too low into the lower orbicularis, especially in patients with poor lower-lid tone.
- **Bruising** — very common here given the vascular, thin skin; usually the most likely (if minor) adverse event.
- **Incomplete or uneven softening** — from mis-spaced points across the fan of lines.

**How it's managed.** As everywhere in this chapter, *prevention by reasoning* is the primary management: superficial placement, lateral and high fields, conservative volume, and screening lower-lid tone at assessment. Zygomaticus-related asymmetry from diffusion is **temporary** and resolves as the effect fades; it is managed with reassurance and documentation, and prevented next time by staying more lateral and superficial. Bruising is managed with the usual measures and expectation-setting. Full algorithms are in **Chapter 8**.

## Practical Competency Checklist (simulated)

> ⚠ **This checklist is for LATER, in-person, supervised sign-off — not self-certification.** Reading this chapter does **not** tick a single box below. They are listed here so you know what competent practice looks like and can rehearse the non-injection elements (consent, positioning, documentation) in simulation now. **The injection steps in particular are acquired only under direct supervision.** This is the Module-1 competency anchor referenced in the blueprint (§8).

**A learner is working toward supervised sign-off on their ability to, for the upper face:**

1. **Prep & asepsis** — perform hand hygiene, cleanse skin per protocol, and maintain aseptic technique throughout; handle product single-patient (Chapter 3).
2. **Equipment & reconstitution** — select the correct Health Canada–approved product, reconstitute aseptically to the intended concentration, and articulate how that dilution affects diffusion (§5.1.3, Chapter 3).
3. **Positioning** — position the patient and themselves for good access, lighting, and ergonomics.
4. **Photography** — capture standardized baseline photographs (consistent angles, lighting, and expression set) before treatment (Chapter 6).
5. **Consent confirmation** — confirm valid informed consent is in place and current, including the specific-risk and no-guarantee framing (Chapter 7), and confirm the **authorization** to treat (order/directive — Chapter 1).
6. **Assessment & marking** — assess dynamic movement, identify the target muscles, note baseline asymmetry, and mark or plan points appropriate to *this* patient's anatomy (individualized, not a fixed template).
7. **Technique per area** — demonstrate depth-, plane-, and placement-reasoning appropriate to the frontalis, glabella, and lateral canthal lines *(acquired under supervision).*
8. **Dose/point accuracy** — draw up and deliver the intended per-point and per-area dose accurately and reproducibly, within the individualized plan *(under supervision).*
9. **Danger-zone adherence** — demonstrably keep diffusion fields away from the levator, the low brow, and zygomaticus; verbalize the danger-zone reasoning while working.
10. **Aftercare counseling** — counsel the patient on aftercare, expected timeline, what to watch for, and how/when to make contact (Chapters 8–9).
11. **Documentation** — record product, lot number, units per point, an injection map, consent, photographs, and any baseline asymmetry to a medico-legal standard (Chapter 9).

## Clinical Pearls

- **The forehead is a balancing act, not a target to abolish.** You are tuning the elevator against the depressors, not switching the frontalis off. Preserve elevation; the brows depend on it.
- **Every glabellar habit points at the septum.** Not too low, not too deep, not too medial-inferior, small contained fields — all of it exists to keep the drug away from the levator.
- **"Superficial, lateral, small" is the whole crow's-feet safety rule.** Memorize the three words; each complication is a violation of one.
- **Dilution is a diffusion decision, not just a reconstitution decision.** Choose your concentration with the danger zone in mind before the needle is ever near the face.
- **Conservative-first plus a two-week review beats chasing perfection on day one.** You can always add; you cannot un-inject.
- **Reading danger zones ≠ being safe around them.** Judgment is what you build here; the motor skill of respecting a danger zone in a live face is built under a proctor.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Thinking in one dose number instead of three.** Per-point, per-area, and per-session are different questions; collapsing them is how over- and under-treatment both happen.
- **Treating the frontalis in isolation.** Ignoring the elevator/depressor balance drops brows and creates peaks.
- **Going too deep in the glabella and crow's feet.** Depth is a safety decision; too deep is how fields reach the levator and the zygomaticus.
- **Over-diluting near the orbit.** More volume means a bigger field exactly where you need the smallest one.
- **Chasing static lines with more units.** BoNT-A softens *movement*; a resting line is a consultation conversation (Chapter 6), not a dosing problem.
- **Treating the checklist as done because you read it.** The competency checklist is a supervised, in-person sign-off — not a reading exercise.

## Summary

The upper face is governed by four principles that repeat across all three areas. **Depth** matches the plane to the muscle and, more importantly, to what lies beneath it — the frontalis over bone, the corrugator deep-to-superficial along its length, the orbicularis very superficial, and the levator hidden deep behind the septum as the structure to protect. **Dose** is three linked concepts — per-point, per-muscle, and per-session total — held together by a conservative-first, review-later philosophy. **Diffusion** is controlled by dilution, volume, and placement, and the core skill is keeping the *edge* of the field away from structures you must not weaken. **Danger zones** name where an escaping field causes harm: the levator (eyelid ptosis), the low-brow frontalis (brow ptosis / "Spock"), and zygomaticus (smile asymmetry). Applied to each area, the frontalis is a balancing act that must preserve brow elevation; the glabella is the highest-stakes area whose every habit protects the levator; and the lateral canthal lines are treated **superficial, lateral, and small** to protect the smile. Doses throughout are **cited educational reference, product-specific, and pending verification — never directives.** And the whole of it is judgment: the motor competency to inject safely is acquired **only** through supervised, in-person, proctored training, confirmed against the competency checklist above. Complication recognition and management are built out in **Chapter 8**; planning, dosing modifiers, and the two-week review in **Chapter 9**.

## Knowledge Check

**1. (Clinical reasoning MCQ) A 58-year-old presents wanting her horizontal forehead lines gone. On assessment you notice her brows sit low and slightly hooded, and she constantly raises her forehead — even at rest — to keep her upper eyelids clear of her lashes. What is the best-reasoned approach to her frontalis?**
A. Treat the frontalis fully to abolish the forehead lines, since that is her stated goal.
B. Treat the frontalis aggressively but low, near the brow, to catch every line.
C. Be very cautious or defer frontalis treatment, because she is using her frontalis to compensate for a heavy/hooded brow, and weakening it could worsen the hooding.
D. Treat only the glabella and never discuss the forehead with her.

> ✅ **Correct: C.** This patient is *frontalis-dependent* — her forehead elevation is compensating for a heavy, hooded brow and low lid position. Weakening the frontalis removes that compensation and can drop the brow further, producing exactly the heavy, tired look she does not want. The best-reasoned approach is caution, patient education, and often deferral or a conservative, high, minimal approach after full discussion. **A** chases line-abolition and ignores function — the classic beginner error. **B** treats *low*, directly weakening the brow-holding fibres — the worst option. **D** abandons the assessment finding and the conversation the patient needs. *Objectives 1 & 5; ties to Chapter 4 (elevator/depressor) and Chapter 6 (assessment).*

**2. (Clinical reasoning MCQ) Ten days after glabellar treatment a patient develops a drooping upper eyelid on one side. Which mechanism best explains this, and which injection-principle failure most likely produced it?**
A. The toxin permanently damaged the eyelid-closing muscle; too little product was used.
B. Product diffused through the orbital septum to the levator palpebrae superioris; the field was likely too low, too deep, or too large near the orbital rim.
C. The frontalis was over-treated laterally; the dilution was too low.
D. The zygomaticus major was weakened; the points were placed too superficially.

> ✅ **Correct: B.** Eyelid ptosis after glabellar treatment is caused by product diffusing to the **levator palpebrae superioris** deep behind the septum — a diffusion-control failure, typically from injecting too low/deep/medial near the rim or using too large a field. **A** is wrong on mechanism (this is temporary chemodenervation, not permanent damage, and the levator — not the eyelid *closer* — is affected) and on cause. **C** describes a *brow*-related mechanism, not eyelid ptosis, and misattributes it. **D** describes the *crow's-feet* smile-asymmetry complication, not eyelid ptosis. *Objectives 3 & 4; ties to Chapter 8.*

**3. (Interpretation MCQ) You are treating crow's feet and want to minimize the risk of an asymmetric smile. Which combination of choices best reflects sound diffusion reasoning?**
A. Deep placement, more anterior toward the cheek, larger volume per point.
B. Superficial placement, kept lateral and higher, small contained fields well-spaced.
C. Superficial placement, but low and anterior over the cheekbone to catch more lines.
D. Deep placement laterally with high dilution to spread the effect widely.

> ✅ **Correct: B.** "Superficial, lateral, small" is the safety logic: superficial matches the thin orbicularis and limits bruising; lateral and higher keeps the field away from **zygomaticus major**; small, well-spaced, contained fields prevent both overlap and unwanted spread. **A** does everything wrong — deep, anterior, and high-volume all drive product toward zygomaticus. **C** is superficial (good) but *low and anterior* (bad), steering the field straight to the danger zone. **D** combines depth and high dilution (large field) laterally — uncontrolled spread. *Objectives 3, 4 & 5.*

**4. (Clinical reasoning MCQ) A colleague reconstitutes a vial with a larger-than-usual diluent volume "to make it go further," then treats the glabella with the resulting product. From a diffusion standpoint, what is the main safety concern?**
A. None — units are units, so dilution has no effect on spread.
B. The higher volume/lower concentration produces a larger diffusion field, which near the orbital rim increases the risk of reaching the levator and causing eyelid ptosis.
C. The product will be too concentrated and cause muscle damage.
D. The larger volume makes the product weaker, so it simply won't work at all.

> ✅ **Correct: B.** Reconstituting in more diluent yields a larger volume at lower concentration, and larger volume generally means a **larger diffusion field** — dangerous in the glabella, where a bigger field is more likely to reach the levator and cause eyelid ptosis. Dilution is a diffusion decision, not just bookkeeping. **A** is the dangerous myth — dilution absolutely affects spread. **C** is backwards (more diluent means *less* concentrated) and misstates the mechanism (chemodenervation, not damage). **D** wrongly implies more diluent destroys potency; the units are the same, but the *field* is bigger. *Objectives 2 & 3; ties to Chapter 3.*

**5. (Clinical reasoning MCQ) A patient with strong "11" frown lines has visible deep vertical creases *at rest*, even when relaxed. She expects the lines to vanish completely after glabellar treatment. What is the most appropriate reasoning-based response?**
A. Increase the glabellar dose well above the usual range to fully erase the static lines.
B. Explain that BoNT-A softens the *dynamic* component; deeply etched static lines will likely improve but may not fully disappear, and over-dosing to chase them raises complication risk without better results — set expectations and consider adjuncts later.
C. Decline all treatment because static lines are an absolute contraindication.
D. Treat the frontalis instead, since it controls the "11" lines.

> ✅ **Correct: B.** Neuromodulators act on *movement*; a line fully etched at rest has a static component BoNT-A cannot fully efface. The right move is expectation-setting: the dynamic component softens, the static line improves but may persist, and pushing the dose to chase it raises risk (including ptosis) without a better outcome. **A** invites complications for no gain. **C** is false — static lines are not a contraindication, just a limitation to discuss. **D** misattributes the "11" lines, which are produced by the **glabellar complex**, not the frontalis. *Objectives 2 & 5; ties to Chapter 6 (assessment) and Chapter 9 (planning/adjuncts).*

**6. (Interpretation / matching-style MCQ) Match the complication to the danger zone whose field was not respected. A patient develops (i) a heavy, low brow, (ii) a drooping upper eyelid, and (iii) a lopsided smile. Which mapping is correct?**
A. (i) zygomaticus, (ii) levator, (iii) low-brow frontalis.
B. (i) low-brow frontalis, (ii) levator, (iii) zygomaticus.
C. (i) levator, (ii) zygomaticus, (iii) low-brow frontalis.
D. (i) low-brow frontalis, (ii) zygomaticus, (iii) levator.

> ✅ **Correct: B.** A heavy, low brow follows weakening the **low-brow frontalis** (the elevator can't hold the brow); a drooping upper eyelid follows the field reaching the **levator** behind the septum; a lopsided smile follows the field reaching **zygomaticus major** below the lateral canthus. **A**, **C**, and **D** scramble the mappings — each swaps at least two mechanisms, and confusing the levator (eyelid) with zygomaticus (smile) or the frontalis (brow) is exactly the reasoning error this item tests. *Objective 4; ties to Chapter 8.*
